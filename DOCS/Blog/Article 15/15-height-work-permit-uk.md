# Height Work Permit UK: Work at Height Regulations 2005 in Practice

*By the MySafeOps team · Last updated 7 June 2026 · 7 min read*

> - WAHR 2005 expects hierarchy of control: avoid, prevent, minimise — permits document the last two when avoidance isn't possible.

Falls from height remain one of the biggest killers in UK construction. The Work at Height Regulations 2005 (WAHR) don't mandate a permit by name, but principal contractors and HSE inspectors routinely expect a **height work permit UK** sites can produce when the method involves fall risk that isn't fully engineered out.

Height work sits in the same family as [hot work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk) and [confined space](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk) inside a proper [permit to work system](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk). The difference is the hazard: uncontrolled gravity rather than fire or atmosphere.

## When you need a height work permit on site

Use a permit when work cannot be done safely from the ground and the remaining controls need explicit authorisation before access. Typical triggers on UK sites:

- Roof work, including fragile or unknown roof coverings
- Leading-edge formwork, decking or cladding installation
- MEWP (mobile elevating work platform) operation in shared areas
- Ladder or tower use where the job isn't a quick, low-risk task
- Steel erection, edge protection installation or removal
- Work over water, plant or live traffic (often combined with other permits)

If edge protection, nets or a MEWP basket already make the task low risk and the RAMS is signed, some firms treat that as RAMS-only. Where multiple trades share the area, weather can change the method, or the edge condition is uncertain, a permit adds a useful second gate.

## What the permit must record

A practical height permit is more than "approved to work at height". Minimum fields:

**Location and task.** Grid line, elevation, what is being installed or repaired.

**Method and equipment.** MEWP type and ground conditions, scaffold tag reference, ladder class and tie pattern, harness and lanyard type if PFPE is in use.

**Exclusion and edge protection.** Who controls the drop zone below, state of guardrails and toe boards, netting where specified.

**Competence.** Named operatives, IPAF card for MEWP, CISRS for scaffolders where relevant, harness competency not assumed from induction alone.

**Weather limits.** Wind speed cut-off for MEWP and crane-suspended platforms, rain affecting fragile roofs or slippery decks.

**Emergency and rescue.** Suspension trauma plan if PFPE is used, ground contact for MEWP emergency lowering, first aider location.

**Duration and handback.** Start time, extension rules, close-out confirming materials secured and holes covered.

Digital permits help at shift handover: the incoming supervisor sees the live permit state instead of hunting a clipboard in the cabin.

## MEWP, scaffolding and fragile surfaces

**MEWP permits** should record ground bearing, outriggers deployed, exclusion zone, operator card, and whether the platform shares airspace with crane lifts or overhead power. A MEWP isn't a substitute for edge protection on a roof — it's a different control set.

**Scaffold access** should reference the current inspection tag and handover note. A height permit doesn't replace the [scaffold inspection register](https://mysafeops.com/blog/scaffold-inspection-checklist-uk); it confirms the team is using tagged scaff only.

**Fragile roofs** need explicit surface identification (asbestos cement, liner panels, rooflights), crawl boards or staging, and a no-walk zone marked on the permit sketch where possible.

## Rescue and suspension trauma

If the method relies on fall-arrest lanyards, the permit must name how a suspended operative will be recovered within the suspension trauma window. "Call 999" is not a plan. Rescue equipment location, trained rescuers and practised drill belong on the permit or linked rescue document.

## Common failures HSE sees

- **Permit signed but edge protection not installed** — the permit becomes paperwork theatre.
- **MEWP used on soft ground without mats** — outrigger load spread missing from the brief.
- **Roof work after rain without re-assessing fragility** — conditions changed; permit wasn't reviewed.
- **Shared permits across days** — each shift or material change needs a fresh authorisation when the method changes.
- **PFPE selected without anchor assessment** — harness on, nowhere safe to tie off.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are work at height permits a legal requirement in the UK?**
WAHR requires planning, competence and suitable equipment. A permit is the usual way UK construction sites prove those controls were applied for high-risk height tasks.

**What's the difference between a RAMS and a height work permit?**
RAMS describes how the job should be done. A permit authorises a specific task, place and time when height risk needs an extra control gate.

**Do I need a permit for a short ladder job?**
Low-risk, short-duration ladder use may be covered in RAMS and toolbox talk alone. If the task is non-routine, above 2 m with no other controls, or in a busy area, use a permit.

**Who should sign a height work permit?**
A competent supervisor who understands the method — often the site manager, appointed person for scaffolding interfaces, or MEWP supervisor. Authorisation must not be a box-tick from someone off site.

**How does digital help with WAHR compliance?**
Timestamped issue, extension and close-out, photos of edge protection, and worker acknowledgements give audit evidence if HSE or a principal contractor asks what was in place on the day.
