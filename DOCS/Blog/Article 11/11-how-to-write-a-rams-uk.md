## What a RAMS actually is (and isn't)

RAMS stands for **Risk Assessment and Method Statement**. It's two documents combined into one, and they do different jobs.

**The risk assessment** identifies what can go wrong with a specific task. It lists hazards, who could be harmed, and what controls are in place. It usually includes a risk matrix scoring likelihood and severity to calculate a risk rating.

**The method statement** describes how the work will be done safely. Step by step. Who does what. What equipment gets used. What checks happen when.

A RAMS without the risk assessment is just a work plan. A RAMS without the method statement is just a hazard list. The two halves matter because planning what to do (method statement) should be informed by knowing what could go wrong (risk assessment). If you've ever seen a RAMS where the method doesn't reflect the hazards identified, you've seen the split break down.

**What RAMS is not:**
- Not legally mandated by name under UK law
- Not a substitute for live site supervision
- Not a one-time document (it needs review if conditions change)
- Not the same as a Construction Phase Plan (that's broader, CDM 2015 specific)
- Not the same as a [permit to work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk) (that's live authorisation, RAMS is planning)

The legal position matters. The Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 requires employers to ensure health and safety of employees "so far as is reasonably practicable". The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 specifically requires risk assessments. CDM 2015 requires principal contractors and contractors to plan construction work properly. RAMS isn't named in any of these, but it's the established way UK construction meets these duties for specific tasks.

## The 10-step process for writing a RAMS

This is the process that produces a RAMS that actually works on site. Every step matters.

### Step 1: Define the task specifically

"Install pipework" is not a task. "Install 110mm stainless steel process pipework supports on Mezzanine level 2, 4.5m above finished floor level, at Two Sisters Scunthorpe RO Room" is a task.

Generic task descriptions lead to generic RAMS. Specificity is what makes the document useful to the crew actually doing the work. Include:
- What's being done
- Where exactly (building, level, zone)
- When (date or phase)
- Duration (estimated days or hours)
- Scope boundaries (what's NOT included)

### Step 2: Identify hazards with real site knowledge

Walk the site if you can. Talk to the person who'll supervise the job. Don't just copy a generic hazard list.

For each sub-task, ask three questions:
1. What could cause harm?
2. Who could be harmed? (Operatives, other trades, members of public, visitors)
3. How could it happen? (Specific scenarios, not abstractions)

**Common UK construction hazards to consider:**
- Manual handling (especially loads over 25kg)
- Working at height (anything where a fall could cause injury)
- Hot works (welding, grinding, cutting)
- Electrical (isolation, proximity to live services)
- Slips, trips and falls (water, cables, debris)
- Falling objects (tools, materials, plant)
- Dust and fumes (silica, welding fumes, bitumen, wood dust)
- Noise (tools, plant, construction activity)
- Vibration (HAVS from power tools)
- Chemicals (solvents, sealants, fuel, cleaning products, plus [COSHH](https://mysafeops.com/blog/coshh-register-software-uk))
- Traffic and plant (on-site vehicle movements, MEWPs, telehandlers)
- Weather (cold, heat, wet, wind for external work)
- Other trades (working alongside, conflicting activities)

### Step 3: Score each hazard with a risk matrix

Most UK construction RAMS uses a Likelihood × Severity = Risk Factor matrix. A common format from live UK sites looks like this:

**Likelihood (L):**
- High (6) – Probable
- Medium (4) – Possible
- Low (2) – Remote

**Severity (S):**
- High (6) – Fatal or major injury
- Medium (4) – Sprain, strain, over 3-day incapacitation
- Low (2) – Cuts and bruises

**Risk Factor (RF) = L × S**

Thresholds:
- **RF > 24:** High risk – eliminate, discuss with manager
- **RF 12-24:** Medium risk – reduce risk, change work programme
- **RF < 12:** Low risk – reduce as far as reasonably practicable

Different companies use different scales (3×3, 5×5 are also common), but the principle is the same. The point isn't the exact number. The point is forcing a judgement on whether the risk is acceptable before work starts, and documenting that judgement.

### Step 4: Define control measures that match the risk

The Health and Safety Executive's **hierarchy of control** tells you the order to think about reducing risk:

1. **Eliminate** the hazard (change the method entirely)
2. **Substitute** with something less hazardous
3. **Engineering controls** (guards, barriers, ventilation)
4. **Administrative controls** (training, procedures, permits)
5. **PPE** (last resort, not first)

Generic RAMS documents jump straight to PPE. That's a failure. Going up the hierarchy usually means safer work and, ironically, easier supervision. A platform that needs full body harness is inherently riskier than a MEWP that removes the fall hazard entirely.

### Step 5: Recalculate the risk after controls

This is the step most RAMS skip. If the original Risk Factor was 24 and you've listed five control measures, what's the new Risk Factor?

A proper RAMS shows **Risk Factor Before** and **Revised Risk Factor After Controls**. If the revised RF is still high, the controls aren't adequate and you need to rethink. If the revised RF is lower, you've documented exactly how the controls reduced the risk.

Example from a real UK site (Two Sisters Scunthorpe, 2026):
- **Task:** Installing mechanical plant above 2000mm
- **Hazard:** Manual handling, falls from heights, falling objects
- **Original RF:** 4 × 4 = **16 (medium risk)**
- **Controls:** Scaffold platform or MEWP, dust mask, gloves, eye protection, clear working area, 110V leads routed safely, no individual lifting above 25kg, IPAF/PASMA qualifications verified
- **Revised RF:** 2 × 6 = **12** (boundary of medium/low)

The residual risk is still there. It's just been reduced to the lowest reasonably practicable level.

### Step 6: Write the method statement

Now comes the how. Step by step. In sequence.

A good method statement for UK construction typically covers:

- **Pre-start checks:** Site access, welfare, permits, inductions
- **Equipment needed:** Tools, plant, materials, PPE
- **Delivery and storage:** Where materials go, how they're protected
- **Task sequence:** Numbered steps, logical order
- **Communication:** Who tells whom, what to do when something changes
- **Breaks and handover:** How work stops and restarts
- **Close-down:** Leaving area safe, signing off permits, waste disposal

Tone matters. Write for the person doing the job, not for the auditor. Short sentences. Concrete actions. Avoid "operatives shall ensure" corporate language when "fit the clamps" works better.

### Step 7: Include a dynamic risk assessment section

Site conditions change. What was safe at 8am may not be safe at 2pm if a roof has come off in a storm, if a subbie has blocked an access route, or if a live service has been exposed by excavation next door.

A good RAMS includes a **dynamic risk assessment** page. Blank. To be filled in on site if new hazards emerge during the work. This is the part that separates a live safety control from a document filed and forgotten.

### Step 8: List PPE and equipment requirements clearly

PPE is last in the hierarchy of control, but it still needs specifying. UK construction typically expects, at minimum:

- Safety footwear (boots or shoes to EN ISO 20345)
- High-visibility vest or jacket (EN ISO 20471)
- Hard hat (EN 397) when at risk of falling objects or head strike

Task-specific additions:
- Gloves to relevant EN standard (EN 388 for mechanical, EN 374 for chemicals)
- Eye protection (EN 166)
- Hearing protection (EN 352) for noise above threshold
- Dust masks (FFP2 or FFP3) for dust exposure
- Harness and lanyard for work at height (EN 361, EN 354)
- Fall arrest systems where edge protection isn't feasible

Specify make, model, or standard. "Safety gloves" isn't enough. "EN 388 4544 mechanical gloves" is.

### Step 9: Signatures and acknowledgement

The RAMS needs to be signed by:

- **Author** (usually the supervisor or lead engineer)
- **Reviewer** (typically principal contractor permit controller or H&S manager)
- **Every operative who will do the work** (acknowledging they've read and understood it)

The operative signatures are what actually matters on site. A RAMS without worker acknowledgement is just paperwork. With signatures, it's evidence that every person on the job knew the hazards and controls.

### Step 10: Set a review date

Every RAMS needs a review date, usually 6-12 months out, or sooner if:
- The task or method changes
- A near-miss or incident occurs
- A new hazard emerges
- Regulation changes (like the current [RIDDOR consultation](https://mysafeops.com/blog/riddor-changes-2026))
- Client or principal contractor requests a review

RAMS from 2019 shouldn't be reused in 2026 without a full review. Site conditions, regulations, PPE standards and best practice all shift.

## The risk matrix: how scoring actually works

Misunderstanding the risk matrix is the single most common RAMS error. Three points worth nailing.

**Likelihood is not the same as frequency.** Likelihood is: how probable is it that this hazard causes harm? A rare hazard with high consequence (a crane collapse) can have higher likelihood score than a common one with low consequence (minor cut).

**Severity scores the worst realistic outcome, not the average.** If a fall from height could kill, the severity is High, even if most falls from that height result in bruises.

**The revised risk factor is not aspirational.** Don't drop the revised RF from 24 to 2 just because you've listed some controls. Be honest. If the fall hazard is still present even after harness + lanyard + rescue plan, the revised RF might only drop from 24 to 12, not to 2.

An overly optimistic revised RF is worse than an honest one. It tells an HSE inspector that the assessor either doesn't understand the risk or is gaming the paperwork.

## Common mistakes that make RAMS useless

After reviewing hundreds of RAMS documents across UK construction sites, the same failures come up again and again.

**Generic copy-paste from another project.** Same hazards, same controls, same wording, different site name. Gets spotted by any competent reviewer and invalidates the document.

**No revised risk factor.** Original RF filled in, control measures listed, revised RF blank. Indicates the assessor didn't actually think about whether the controls are adequate.

**PPE as the only control.** If the document jumps straight to "wear gloves and hi-vis" without engineering or administrative controls, the assessor hasn't applied the hierarchy of control.

**No worker signatures.** Supervisor signed, reviewer signed, nobody on the crew signed. The people doing the work haven't acknowledged the hazards.

**Review date in the past.** Document written in 2022, review due 2024, still in use in 2026. The RAMS is out of date before it's opened.

**Method statement disconnected from risk assessment.** The hazards list says "working at height over 2m", the method statement says "operative stands on milk crate". Classic disconnect.

**Length without substance.** 40 pages of generic text, 2 pages of actual site-specific content. Nobody reads it, everyone signs it, nothing gets controlled.

**No dynamic risk assessment section.** No mechanism for capturing new hazards found on site. The RAMS can't evolve with conditions.

## When to write a task-specific RAMS

Not every task needs a bespoke RAMS. Writing a dedicated RAMS for hanging a picture wastes time. Writing a generic RAMS for confined space entry gets people killed.

**Always write a task-specific RAMS for:**
- High-risk activities (confined space, hot works, work at height over 2m, electrical isolation, lifting operations, excavation)
- Work requiring a [permit to work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk)
- Complex multi-trade coordination
- Unusual or first-time methods
- When client or principal contractor specifically requests one
- Subcontracted work where the subbie provides their own RAMS

**A generic task RAMS may be acceptable for:**
- Low-risk routine tasks (office-based work, supervision only, basic material handling)
- Repeat visits to the same site doing the same work under the same conditions

Even for "generic" scenarios, a site-specific front sheet (location, date, names, key hazards for this visit) is still needed. A fully generic RAMS with no site detail is almost always a red flag.

- Company name and logo

- Reviewed by (name, position, signature, date)
- Review due date

**Page 2: Task Team and Sign-On**
- List of operatives assigned
- Date, printed name, signature for each
- Confirmation that RAMS has been read and understood
- Key responsibilities listed

**Page 3-5: Risk Assessment Tables**
For each sub-task:
- Activity
- Hazards identified
- Who might be harmed
- Risk Factor (L × S = RF) before controls
- Control measures
- Revised Risk Factor after controls

**Page 6-8: Method Statement**
- Pre-start checks
- Equipment and PPE
- Step-by-step task sequence
- Communication plan
- Breaks and handover
- Close-down procedures

**Page 9: PPE and Equipment Schedule**
- Minimum PPE requirements
- Task-specific PPE with standards
- Equipment required with calibration/inspection status

**Page 10: Dynamic Risk Assessment**
- Blank page for site-specific additions during work
- Space for photos, sketches, notes

**Page 11: Acknowledgement and Sign-Off**
- Operative sign-off confirming RAMS read and understood
- Supervisor confirmation that RAMS briefing has been delivered

**Page 12: Emergency Procedures**
- First aid arrangements
- Assembly point
- Accident reporting procedure
- Key phone numbers

## Moving from paper RAMS to digital

Paper RAMS works, up to a point. The point where it breaks is usually around the time a subbie turns up on site at 7am and the RAMS hasn't been issued yet because the paperwork is still in the office.

Digital RAMS fixes three specific failure points of paper:

**Version control.** The RAMS on the supervisor's phone is always the latest approved version. No risk of working from a superseded document.

**Worker sign-off.** Operatives sign on their own phone with a QR code. No chasing signatures on a clipboard. Audit trail is time-stamped and geo-tagged.

**Review reminders.** The system flags RAMS due for review, so nothing stays in use past its review date by accident.

Platforms where worker accounts are free permanently (like MySafeOps) remove the usual cost barrier to getting operatives onto the digital workflow. On a site of 50+ workers, per-seat charges for RAMS access turn digital into a luxury. Free worker access makes it standard.

The transition from paper to digital RAMS usually takes a UK contractor about 4-6 weeks. Start with high-risk activities (permits, hot works, confined space), migrate low-risk routine tasks later.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a RAMS legally required in the UK?**
Not by name. But the risk assessment duty under the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 and the planning duty under CDM 2015 effectively require equivalent documentation. UK construction clients and principal contractors routinely request RAMS before granting site access, which makes them practically necessary regardless of legal status.

**What's the difference between a RAMS and a Method Statement alone?**
A Method Statement describes how work will be done. A RAMS combines that with a formal risk assessment showing hazards, controls and scoring. A standalone Method Statement without risk assessment is usually not accepted by UK construction principal contractors.

**How long should a RAMS be?**
Task-dependent. A simple repeat task might be 4-6 pages. A complex multi-trade installation might be 12-20 pages. What matters is relevance, not length. A 4-page site-specific RAMS beats a 40-page generic one every time.

**Who writes the RAMS?**
Usually the supervisor, lead engineer or H&S manager responsible for the work. Input from the crew who'll carry out the task is critical. RAMS written in isolation by an office-based H&S coordinator who's never seen the site tend to be detached from site reality.

**How often should a RAMS be reviewed?**
Minimum annually, or whenever the task, method, site conditions or regulations change. After any near-miss or incident related to the task, the RAMS should be reviewed and updated.

**Can I reuse a RAMS from another project?**
Only as a starting template. The site-specific content (location, access, surrounding activities, specific hazards) must be updated for every new project. Full copy-paste reuse is a common audit finding and creates liability if something goes wrong.

**Do subcontractors need their own RAMS?**
Yes, for their specific tasks. The principal contractor may provide a template or require alignment with their own RAMS structure, but the subbie is responsible for the RAMS covering work they carry out. Overlapping RAMS from different trades on the same area need coordination to avoid conflicts.

**What happens if an HSE inspector reviews my RAMS?**
They'll typically check: is it specific to the task, are hazards realistically identified, are controls adequate, is the method achievable, are operatives signed on, is the review date current. Red flags are generic content, missing signatures, unrealistic risk scoring, and outdated revision numbers.

## Get the RAMS right, the rest of the paperwork follows

A RAMS that actually works on site does three things. It makes the crew safer because the hazards and controls are clear. It protects the business because the evidence trail is structured. And it keeps the client happy because the document reflects real thought about real risk. Getting that right takes a decent template, 30-60 minutes of effort per task, and the discipline to review when conditions change. The rest is habit.
