Roughly 50% of fatal injuries in UK construction come from falls from height. HSE statistics from the past five years put scaffolding incidents near the top of that list, usually linked to incomplete erection, missing components, or use of scaffolds that had not been inspected after modification.

The legal framework around **scaffold inspection UK** construction work isn't complicated, but it catches sites out when the paperwork slips. This guide walks through what the regulations actually require, what a good scaffold inspection looks like in practice, and how to keep a register that passes both a weekly check and an HSE audit.

## The UK legal framework for scaffold inspection

Three pieces of legislation set the rules.

**Work at Height Regulations 2005.** The primary framework. Applies to all work where a fall could cause injury, regardless of height. Requires work at height to be properly planned, supervised and carried out by competent people with appropriate equipment.

**Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015 (CDM 2015).** Sets broader duties for planning and managing construction work. Principal Contractors and contractors both have responsibilities for scaffold use on their projects.

**Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998 (PUWER).** Scaffolds are work equipment. PUWER requires equipment to be suitable, maintained, inspected and used only by those trained to use it.

Regulation 12 of Work at Height Regulations 2005 is the key section for scaffold inspection specifically. It requires:

- Inspection after erection or assembly
- Inspection after any event likely to have affected stability (high winds, impact, alterations)
- Inspection at suitable intervals, and specifically **not exceeding 7 days** for scaffolds used in construction
- Results of inspection recorded and kept available on site

The 7-day rule is the one most sites operate against. Every scaffold structure in use needs a weekly inspection, minimum, with the results recorded.

## Who counts as a competent scaffold inspector

HSE guidance is clear on this: competence depends on knowledge, training and experience appropriate to the type and complexity of the scaffold being inspected.

**For complex scaffolds** (bridges, cantilevers, temporary roofs, loading bays, scaffolds outside standard configurations):
- CISRS Advanced Scaffolder, or
- CISRS Scaffold Inspection Advanced (SIA), or
- Equivalent specific training from scaffold designer

**For standard basic scaffolds** (tube-and-fitting or system scaffolds to a recognised standard configuration):
- CISRS Scaffolder, or
- CISRS Scaffold Inspection Basic (SIB), or
- Site manager or supervisor who has completed a recognised scaffold inspection course

**For mobile tower scaffolds:**
- PASMA certified user (minimum)
- Or equivalent manufacturer-specific training

The inspector must be demonstrably competent for the **specific scaffold being inspected**. A site manager with basic scaffold inspection training can sign off a simple tube-and-fitting scaffold against a standard configuration. They cannot competently inspect a cantilever bridge or a complex loading bay.

Getting this wrong is a common HSE enforcement theme. A site manager signed off a scaffold inspection that included a non-standard hanging deck. When the deck failed, the prosecution focused on competence: the inspector was qualified for basic scaffolds, not for the complex feature they'd signed off.

## The four inspection triggers in UK law

UK regulations require scaffold inspection at four specific trigger points. Missing any of them creates a compliance gap.

**Trigger 1: After erection, before first use.**
The scaffold must be inspected after it's built and before anyone uses it. This applies to both full-scale scaffolds and mobile towers. The inspection confirms the structure is safe to use for its intended purpose.

**Trigger 2: After any event that could affect stability.**
High winds (Met Office warnings), impact from vehicles or materials, prolonged rain on soft ground, adjacent excavations, alterations by another trade. All trigger a re-inspection before use resumes.

**Trigger 3: After alteration, modification or partial dismantling.**
Any change to the scaffold, from adding a single board to removing part of a bay, requires re-inspection. This is where most breaches happen. A trade adjusts a bay to fit around plant, the change never gets signed off, and the next inspection finds the alteration wasn't compliant.

**Trigger 4: At intervals not exceeding 7 days.**
The weekly inspection is the default ongoing requirement. Sites where scaffolds are in continuous use need a weekly register showing inspection every 7 days or less, documented with the inspector's name, signature, date and time.

## What a proper weekly scaffold inspection actually covers

A decent scaffold inspection for UK construction work goes through about 30 specific check points. The structure below follows what an HSE inspector or Principal Contractor auditor would look for.

### Pre-inspection preparation
- Permit or authorisation to inspect
- Appropriate PPE (hard hat, hi-vis, safety footwear, gloves, fall protection if needed)
- Weather conditions suitable (no high winds during inspection)
- Previous inspection register and any relevant drawings

### Ground conditions and foundations
- Base plates present and seated on firm level ground
- Sole boards used where needed on soft ground
- No water pooling around base
- No undermining or settlement visible
- Standards vertical, no lean

### Standards (uprights)
- Correct spacing per design or standard configuration
- Joint pins used and fully seated
- No damaged, bent or corroded tubes
- Standards plumb (vertical)
- Extended above top deck to support guardrails

### Ledgers and transoms
- Level and properly secured with right-angle couplers
- Bracing present and correctly fitted
- No missing, loose or damaged couplers
- Nuts tightened appropriately

### Bracing
- Facade bracing at required intervals
- Ledger bracing at both ends and at required intervals
- Plan bracing if required
- Bracing connected with the correct couplers (swivel where needed)

### Ties to the building
- Correct type and frequency per design
- Tie checks logged and in date
- No missing or defective ties
- Through-ties not obstructed or overloaded

### Platforms (working decks)
- Fully boarded across the width specified in design
- Boards in good condition (no splits, warping or water damage)
- Boards securely fixed (no trap ends beyond 150mm or under 50mm overhang)
- Correct number of boards for duty rating (light, general, heavy)

### Guardrails and toe boards
- Top guardrail at 950mm minimum
- Intermediate guardrail fitted
- Toe boards at least 150mm high, properly secured
- Brick guard or mesh where required for materials

### Access and ladders
- Dedicated access ladder or stairway
- Ladder secured top and bottom
- Ladder extended 1m above platform or with suitable handhold
- Safe transition from ladder to platform

### Signs and tags
- Scaffold tag or status board visible
- Current inspection status clear
- "Do Not Use" clearly marked if scaffold is incomplete or failed inspection
- Loading or duty rating displayed

### Environmental and specific risks
- Adequate lighting for use
- Sheeting or debris netting fitted correctly if specified
- Electrical hazards checked (distance from overhead lines, lighting)
- Public protection where scaffold borders pedestrian or vehicle routes

Any failure against these points either gets immediate remedial action (and re-inspection) or the scaffold is taken out of use until fixed.

## Scaffold tag systems (Scafftag, Scaffalert and others)

Most UK construction sites use a visual tag system to communicate inspection status at the point of access to the scaffold. The dominant system in the UK is **Scafftag**, with alternatives from Scaffalert and manufacturer-specific systems.

**How scaffold tag systems work:**
- Inspection status is indicated by a coloured insert or panel
- Green (or similar) = inspection current, safe to use
- Red (or similar) = inspection failed or overdue, do not use
- Tamper-evident seals confirm the checklist insert hasn't been removed or altered
- Duplicate carbon copy of the inspection form stays in the tag, with copy in register

**Physical tag systems work well when:**
- Sites have multiple scaffold structures
- Multiple trades use the same scaffold
- Handover is documented physically

**Where they fall short:**
- Tags get damaged, lost or stolen
- Illegible handwriting on carbon inserts
- Out-of-date tags not spotted on busy sites
- Records are on paper, inaccessible remotely
- Tamper seals don't prevent anyone from ignoring the tag

Digital scaffold registers address these issues by linking the physical scaffold to a digital inspection record accessible on any device. A QR code on the scaffold can link directly to the current inspection status, photos from the last inspection, and upcoming re-inspection due dates.

## What to do when inspection fails

If a scaffold inspection reveals a defect, the action depends on severity.

**Minor defects** (missing toe board section, loose coupler on a non-critical component, damaged board that isn't load-bearing): fix immediately, re-inspect the affected area, record the issue in the register.

**Major defects** (missing ties, incorrect bracing, damaged standards, inadequate guardrails, overloaded decks): scaffold is taken out of use. "Do Not Use" tag or sign applied. Access blocked physically if needed. Scaffold contractor or designer engaged to assess and remediate. Full re-inspection before use resumes.

**Critical defects** (missing fundamental structural elements, visible movement, signs of collapse risk): site clear the area, exclusion zone established, scaffold not approached until structural engineer or scaffold designer has assessed.

For any defect, the record stays in the register. Incidents involving scaffold failure trigger reportable dangerous occurrence status under [RIDDOR 2013](https://mysafeops.com/blog/riddor-changes-2026). The proposed 2026 RIDDOR changes (currently under consultation) would explicitly broaden this to include trench collapse and temporary works failure, strengthening the requirement for comprehensive records.

## Digital vs paper scaffold registers

Paper scaffold registers work until they don't. The failure modes are familiar to any UK site manager who has run a busy project.

**Paper register problems:**
- Register lost between site and office
- Handwriting illegible after rain
- Difficult to match photos to specific inspections
- Auditing across multiple scaffolds is slow
- Out-of-date registers keep circulating after updates
- Subcontractor inspections sometimes don't make it into the main register

**Digital register advantages:**
- Photos attached to specific inspection points
- GPS location tied to each inspection
- Automatic reminder when next inspection is due
- Instant export for HSE or client audits
- Remote visibility for office-based H&S managers
- Subcontractor inspections integrated into single view

A digital register doesn't replace the physical presence of a competent inspector on the scaffold. What it does is remove the friction around documenting the inspection properly. On a multi-scaffold site, the time saving adds up to several hours per week across a supervision team.

For small contractors with one or two scaffolds, paper works fine. For sites with 5+ scaffold structures, multiple subcontractors, or complex structures, digital registers materially reduce administrative burden and audit risk.

## Free scaffold inspection template structure

Here's the template structure to use as a starting point. Copy the sections, customise for your scaffold type, use consistently across projects.

### Header section
- Project name and location
- Scaffold identifier (unique reference)
- Date and time of inspection
- Type of inspection (initial/weekly/post-event/post-modification)
- Weather conditions at time of inspection

### Scaffold details
- Scaffold type (tube and fitting, system, mobile tower)
- Duty rating (light/general/heavy/special purpose)
- Purpose of scaffold
- Design or standard configuration reference
- Scaffold contractor

### Inspection checklist
The 30+ point checklist from the section above, grouped into:
- Ground conditions and foundations
- Standards and ledgers
- Bracing
- Ties
- Platforms
- Guardrails and toe boards
- Access
- Signage and tags

Each point: Pass / Fail / N/A + notes field + photo attachment option

### Defects register
For each failure:
- Description of defect
- Severity (minor / major / critical)
- Immediate action taken
- Further action required
- Person responsible for remediation
- Target completion date
- Re-inspection required (yes/no)

### Inspector sign-off
- Inspector name (printed)
- CISRS card number or qualification reference
- Position/role
- Signature
- Date and time

### Re-inspection due
- Next scheduled inspection date
- Reason for interval (weekly default, or specified period)
- Reminder trigger set (for digital registers)

### Appendix (optional)
- Photos of scaffold from multiple angles
- Copy of relevant design drawings
- Copy of scaffold handover certificate

## Frequently asked questions

**How often must UK scaffolding be inspected?**
After erection before first use, after any event affecting stability, after any modification, and at intervals not exceeding 7 days. The 7-day rule is the key ongoing requirement for scaffolds in continuous use.

**Can a site manager inspect a scaffold without being a CISRS scaffolder?**
For basic scaffolds to a standard configuration, a site manager with recognised scaffold inspection training can competently inspect. For complex scaffolds (cantilevers, bridges, temporary roofs, non-standard configurations), CISRS Advanced Scaffolder or Scaffold Inspection Advanced certification is typically required.

**What's the difference between Work at Height Regulations and scaffold-specific rules?**
Work at Height Regulations 2005 is the broad framework covering all work at height. Regulation 12 specifically requires scaffold inspection. Related guidance comes from HSE and trade bodies like NASC (National Access and Scaffolding Confederation) which publishes SG4 guidance on safe scaffolding practices.

**How long do scaffold inspection records need to be kept?**
Until the scaffold is dismantled and removed, plus a minimum of 3 months after. Many UK firms keep records for longer (1-7 years) for audit and civil liability purposes.

**What's a Scafftag or similar tag system?**
A physical tag attached to the scaffold showing current inspection status. Green insert = inspection current, safe to use. Red = failed or overdue. Carbon-copy checklist inserts document the inspection details. Common in UK construction for point-of-use status communication.

**Do mobile tower scaffolds need the same inspection as full scaffolds?**
Yes. Work at Height Regulations 2005 applies equally. Mobile towers must be inspected after assembly, before use, after any event affecting stability, and at intervals not exceeding 7 days if in continuous use.

**What happens if an HSE inspector finds a scaffold without inspection records?**
Typical outcome depends on severity. Minor gaps might result in a notice of contravention or improvement notice. Significant gaps (no register at all, or evidence of dangerous use) can result in prohibition notices, fees for intervention (currently £174 per hour of HSE time), and in serious cases, prosecution under Work at Height Regulations 2005 or HSWA 1974.

**Can a scaffold subcontractor refuse to work on an unsafe scaffold?**
Yes. Workers have legal rights under HSWA 1974 to refuse work they reasonably believe is unsafe. The scaffold hirer/user is responsible for ensuring scaffolds are inspected before use. Subcontractors should verify current inspection status before accessing.
