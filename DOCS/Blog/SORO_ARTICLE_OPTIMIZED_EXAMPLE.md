# Permit to Work System for UK Construction Sites: What Actually Works

*By the MySafeOps team · Last updated 16 April 2026 · 7 min read*

A hot works contractor is ready to start, the RAMS have been briefed, and the job looks straightforward. Then someone spots that the area has changed since the last walk-round. New materials stored nearby, a live service route now exposed, and another trade working within the exclusion zone. This is exactly where **a permit to work system** matters. It creates a control point before high-risk activity begins, so work starts under the right conditions rather than on assumptions.

In construction, that control point is not just paperwork. It is a live authorisation process used to confirm the task, the location, the hazards, the precautions, the people involved and the person accountable for approval. When the system is working properly, it reduces ambiguity on site. When it is weak, teams end up relying on memory, verbal sign-off or outdated forms. None of which stands up well under pressure, or under scrutiny after an incident.

## What a permit to work system actually does

A permit to work system is a formal method for managing higher-risk activities that need tighter control than a standard task briefing alone. It does not replace a risk assessment or RAMS. It sits alongside them and confirms that the job can proceed at that moment, in that place, under those conditions.

That distinction matters. RAMS say how work should be done in principle. A permit checks whether the site is actually ready for it now. On a live construction project, conditions shift constantly. Access routes change, plant movements increase, segregations are altered, services are uncovered and work fronts overlap. A permit process gives supervisors and authorised persons a structured way to stop, check and approve.

For most UK contractors, permits are used for [hot works](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk), [confined spaces](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk), electrical isolation, excavations, work at height in sensitive areas, lifting operations or entry into restricted zones. The exact scope varies by company and project. What matters is that permit-required work is clearly defined and consistently controlled.

The HSE's guidance on electronic permit to work systems (HSG250) sets out what a well-designed PTW process should cover. It doesn't mandate a specific format. It does make clear that whatever system you run, it needs to produce traceable evidence of authorisation, competent sign-off and proper close-out.

## Why permit systems break down on site

The failure point is rarely the idea of permits. It is usually the execution. Many UK sites still operate with paper forms, disconnected approvals and inconsistent handovers between office and field teams. A permit may be issued, but not visible to the supervisor carrying out the task. It may be signed, but without evidence that isolations were confirmed or the area inspected. It may expire, yet work continues because no one has a clear prompt to review it.

There is also a behavioural issue. If the process is slow, repetitive or difficult to access, people start treating it as admin rather than a safety control. That is when permits become a box-ticking exercise. Supervisors rush them, managers sign them remotely without full context, and subcontractors see them as delay rather than protection.

A permit system only works if it is practical enough for live site conditions. It needs clear responsibilities, quick access, visible status and a straightforward approval route. The best systems support control without creating unnecessary friction.

## The core elements of an effective permit system

A good permit process is precise. It should identify the exact task, location, time period, responsible contractor, issuing authority and required precautions. It should also show the checks completed before authorisation, such as isolations, atmosphere testing, fire controls, temporary works review or exclusion arrangements, depending on the task.

Just as important is the handback. Too many permit processes focus on issue but not closure. Once work is complete, the permit should confirm the area is left safe, temporary controls have been removed or maintained as required, and any plant, services or systems are returned to the correct status. Without that final step, sites carry residual risk long after the job appears finished.

Competence sits at the centre of the system. A permit is not valid simply because a form exists. It is only meaningful if the people issuing, receiving and monitoring it understand the hazards and their authority. On construction sites with multiple subcontractors, that means role clarity matters. Who can request? Who can approve? Who can suspend? Who can close? If those answers are vague, control is weak.

## Permit controls should match real site risk

Not every activity needs the same level of permit detail. Overcomplicating low-risk work can overload the system and reduce attention where it matters most. Equally, under-controlling genuinely hazardous tasks creates obvious exposure.

The right balance depends on the project, the work package and the surrounding environment. Hot works in a clean, isolated fabrication area present a different control challenge from hot works in a partially occupied refurbishment with poor segregation and combustible materials nearby. The permit process should reflect that difference rather than forcing every task through the same generic route.

This is where HSE guidance on confined space work (ACOP L101) is useful. It makes clear that the permit-to-enter is not a procedural formality. It's the documented check that rescue arrangements, atmospheric testing and communication are all genuinely in place before anyone goes in. Treating every permit as equivalent in depth misses that point.

## Digital permit to work benefits in construction

UK construction firms are moving away from paper permits for a simple reason: paper struggles on dynamic sites. Documents go missing, revisions are unclear, signatures are hard to verify and the latest approval status is not always visible to the people who need it. That creates both compliance risk and operational delay.

A digital permit to work system improves control because information is available in real time. Site managers can see what has been requested, what has been approved, what is active and what has expired. Supervisors can access permits on a mobile phone at the point of work rather than chasing folders or ringing the office. Health and safety teams can review records without waiting for paperwork to be returned from site.

There is also a stronger audit trail. Time-stamped approvals, named users, attached evidence and clear version history make it easier to show who authorised what and when. For businesses managing multiple projects, that consistency is valuable. It standardises permit discipline across sites without removing project-level control.

This is where mobile-first systems are especially useful. If permit checks can only be completed from a desktop in the site office, the process still drifts away from the actual workface. When approvals, reviews and status checks happen from a worker's own phone, the system is more likely to be used properly in the field, where the risk sits. ## How to improve your permit system

If your permit process feels heavy but still leaves gaps, the answer is not usually more forms. It is better control design. Start by looking at where permits fail in practice. Are requests unclear? Are approvers too remote from the work area? Are permits issued without evidence? Are handbacks inconsistent? Fixing those weak points is more effective than adding more signatures.

Then review the trigger points. Teams should know exactly when a permit is required and what must be in place before one can be issued. That includes linked documents such as RAMS, competency records, isolation certificates or inspection evidence where relevant. If permit issue depends on information spread across email chains, WhatsApp messages and paper files, mistakes become more likely.

It also helps to tighten visibility. Active permits should be easy to identify, especially where several trades are operating at once. Site leadership needs a clear view of concurrent high-risk work, because the risk often comes from overlap rather than from one task in isolation.

## Common signs your permit process needs attention

You do not need a major incident to know the system is under strain. Warning signs usually appear earlier. Permits are being backdated, work starts before authorisation, closure records are incomplete, or supervisors rely on verbal confirmation because the paperwork is not readily available. None of these issues are minor. They show the control is no longer functioning reliably under real site conditions.

That is the key test. A permit system should hold up on a busy day, with changing site access, pressure on programme and multiple subcontractors competing for workface. If it only works when everything is quiet, it is not strong enough.

## Permit systems and legal accountability

A permit itself does not guarantee compliance. It is one control within a wider safety management framework. But if a serious event occurs, the quality of the permit process will be examined closely. Investigators will look at whether the hazard was understood, whether precautions were suitable, whether authorisation was competent and whether the work was monitored.

For principal contractors and duty holders under CDM 2015, that means permits are not just operational documents. They are evidence of control. A poor permit record can suggest weak supervision, fragmented communication or ineffective planning. A strong one shows that risk-critical decisions were structured, visible and accountable.

The best permit systems are neither bureaucratic nor casual. They are disciplined. They give site teams a workable process for stopping unsafe starts, verifying conditions and recording who made the call.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a permit to work system legally required under UK law?**
There's no single regulation that mandates a permit system by name, but HSWA 1974, CDM 2015 and activity-specific regulations (Confined Spaces 1997, Work at Height 2005, Electricity at Work 1989) together require controlled authorisation for high-risk work. A permit system is the established means of meeting those duties.

**What's the difference between a permit to work and a RAMS?**
RAMS (risk assessment and method statement) describes how the work should be carried out in principle. A permit confirms that the specific conditions on site right now are suitable for that work to proceed. RAMS are planning documents. Permits are live authorisations.

**Are digital permits accepted by HSE inspectors?**
Yes. HSE guidance HSG250 explicitly recognises electronic permit to work systems where they provide equivalent evidence to paper permits (authorisation, traceability, signatures, close-out). Digital PTW is increasingly the expectation on larger UK construction projects.

**How long is a permit usually valid for?**
Most UK permits are issued for a single shift or a specific time window (commonly 8-12 hours), with re-validation required if work extends. Some lower-risk permits may run up to 7 days. Time-limited permits force re-checking of conditions, which is the point.

**Who should issue permits on a construction site?**
A competent authorised person, typically a supervisor, site manager or permit controller with specific training for the permit type. The issuer must be separate from the person carrying out the work. On complex projects, the principal contractor usually designates named permit authorisers by permit category.

**What happens if a permit expires while work is still ongoing?**
Work stops. Conditions are re-checked. A new permit (or a properly documented extension, where the system allows) is issued before work resumes. Work continuing past permit expiry is one of the most common audit findings and is exactly what good digital systems flag automatically.
