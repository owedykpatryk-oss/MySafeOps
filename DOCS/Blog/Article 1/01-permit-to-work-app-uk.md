## What a permit to work actually does on site

A permit to work is a controlled authorisation for high-risk activity. It's not admin. It's the document that says: this work, at this location, by this person, with these controls in place, is safe to start now.

On paper this sounds simple. In practice it's where site safety quietly breaks down. A permit gets issued on Monday and forgotten by Wednesday. A subcontractor changes scope without anyone re-validating the controls. The permit board hasn't been updated since the last inspection. And when something goes wrong, nobody can prove who authorised what.

A digital permit does the same job but keeps the evidence trail alive. Every action is timestamped. Every signature is captured. Every close-out check either happened or didn't. That's the real upgrade over paper, and it's why the Health and Safety Executive accepts electronic PTW systems in UK construction when they're set up properly.

## The four UK permits most construction sites need

Every site's permit list looks slightly different, but four types come up on almost every UK construction job. A decent app covers all four out of the box.

[**Hot work permits**](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk) control anything that creates heat, sparks or flame. Welding, grinding, cutting, bitumen boilers, blow torches. The permit confirms the area is clear, fire watch is in place, and suitable extinguishers are to hand. Hot work is the single biggest cause of avoidable fire damage on construction sites, so this permit carries weight with insurers as well as the HSE.

**Working at height permits** cover anything off ground level where a fall could cause injury. Scaffold access, MEWP operation, fragile roof work, leading edge work. The permit ties back to the Work at Height Regulations 2005 and forces the right questions: is the hierarchy of controls applied, is edge protection in place, is the operator trained and competent.

[**Confined space permits**](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk) apply to tanks, pits, deep excavations, roof voids, chambers and any enclosed space where the atmosphere might be dangerous. These carry the highest fatality rate of any UK construction permit category. The permit confirms atmospheric testing, rescue arrangements, communication plan and time limits.

**Electrical permits** control work on or near live electrical systems. Lockout/tagout, isolation verification, competent person sign-off. These permits are where most sites under-document, because the work feels routine.

A good permit to work app doesn't just digitise these four. It keeps the specifics of each, because a hot work permit needs different fields from a confined space one, and treating them identically is how controls get missed.

## What to look for in a permit to work app

Not all PTW software is built the same, and a lot of it is sold by companies who've never stood on a live site. Here's what actually matters when you're choosing.

**Permit lifecycle, not just a form.** Issuing is only the start. The app should handle approval routing, active status, pause/resume, expiry alerts, close-out checks and archive. If it just creates a PDF and emails it, that's a form builder, not a permit system.

**Mobile first, not mobile-adapted.** Most site staff will use a phone. If the app feels like a desktop tool squeezed onto a screen, adoption will collapse inside a month. Test it on a phone before you buy.

**Offline capability for real sites.** Basements, tunnels, steel-framed buildings, rural sites. Signal drops constantly on UK construction. The app needs to queue permits locally and sync when signal returns. Anything less is a desk toy.

**Digital signatures with audit trail.** When the HSE asks who signed what, when, the answer should be in the app in five seconds. Paper signatures with a phone photo don't cut it.

**Worker access without a per-seat cost.** Operatives need to sign permits and acknowledge briefings. Charging £10 a month per worker so they can tap 'accept' is the fastest way to stop adoption dead. Platforms that keep worker accounts free (like MySafeOps) remove that friction entirely.

**Close-out evidence.** A permit that never gets properly closed is worse than paper, because it looks like you had a system. The app should force close-out questions and photos, not just let permits drift into history.

## Offline-first is not optional

This deserves its own point because it's where most PTW apps fall over.

A permit to work app that needs full 4G to open isn't fit for construction. Think about the places permits get issued: inside plant rooms, below ground level, on rural civil sites, at 7am when every operative is hammering the masts. If the app can't let a supervisor issue a [confined space permit](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk) standing next to the confined space, it's the wrong tool.

Good PTW software stores permits locally on the device, lets them be completed offline, and syncs the whole record (with timestamps preserved) when connectivity returns. MySafeOps is built this way because the team spent time on actual UK sites before writing the first line of code.

## Common mistakes when going digital with PTW

Teams that struggle with digital permits usually make the same handful of mistakes.

They **pick a platform without involving site staff.** The office signs up, the site doesn't adopt, and three months later the spreadsheet is back. The fix is obvious: test any PTW app with a real supervisor and a real operative before you sign a contract.

They **over-configure the workflow.** Complex approval chains look impressive in a demo. On a live site they cause delays, workarounds and eventually resentment. Start simple. Add layers only when a specific problem demands it.

They **digitise broken processes.** If the permit process was confused on paper, inconsistent close-outs, unclear authority, missing records, digitising it just makes the mess searchable. Fix the process first, then apply software.

They **forget about the evidence.** A permit system that's easy to issue but hard to audit fails at the worst possible moment. Check how reports export and how quickly you can pull six months of permits for a client audit.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are digital permits to work legally accepted in the UK?**
Yes. HSE guidance (HSG250) recognises electronic permit to work systems where they provide equivalent control and evidence to paper permits. That means proper authorisation, traceability, signatures and close-out records.

**Do I need a separate app for each permit type?**
No. A proper permit to work app handles all UK permit categories (hot work, height, confined space, electrical, excavation, lifting, general) in one place. Splitting them across apps creates exactly the admin burden digital PTW is meant to remove.

**How long does it take to roll out a PTW app on site?**
With a decent platform, a small contractor can be issuing live digital permits inside a week. Larger firms with complex approval chains usually need 2-4 weeks to configure and train. The platforms that take six months to deploy are flagging something about their complexity.

**What happens to permits if the app goes offline?**
Proper offline-capable PTW apps store permits locally on the device. The supervisor can issue, sign and close permits without signal. When the device reconnects, everything syncs with original timestamps preserved. If an app can't do this, it's not ready for UK construction.

**Do workers need their own licence to sign permits?**
They shouldn't. Platforms that charge per-worker make digital adoption painful for large teams. MySafeOps keeps worker accounts free permanently, so everyone on site can sign briefings and acknowledge permits without a cost barrier.
