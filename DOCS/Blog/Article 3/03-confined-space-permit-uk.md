Confined space incidents don't give second chances. Most UK construction sites that deal with tanks, chambers, pits, deep excavations or roof voids already know this. The statistics are brutal: the majority of confined space fatalities involve someone going in to rescue the first casualty without proper equipment. The entry that looked safe enough to attempt is the same entry that kills the rescuer.

This is why **a confined space permit UK regulations require** isn't really about entry. It's about what happens if something goes wrong.

It sits alongside other high-risk permits in a typical [permit to work system](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk). For contrast, [hot work permits](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk) address fire risk in the open air or buildings, not atmospheric hazards in enclosed spaces, so the fields on the form need to stay distinct.

## What counts as a confined space in UK construction

The Confined Spaces Regulations 1997 define a confined space as any place that is substantially enclosed where there's a foreseeable risk of serious injury from hazardous conditions. The definition isn't about size. It's about the conditions inside.

On UK construction sites, that typically means:

- Water tanks, storage tanks and fuel tanks
- Manholes, chambers and drainage systems
- Deep excavations (usually over 1.2m with restricted access)
- Sealed or partially sealed roof voids
- Ductwork and large HVAC systems
- Tunnels and service shafts
- Cellars and basements with limited ventilation
- Enclosed plant rooms during commissioning

The hazards the regulations care about are specific: flammable gas or fumes, toxic atmosphere, oxygen deficiency, oxygen enrichment, engulfment by free-flowing solids, heat causing heat stroke, and restricted access preventing rescue.

A space that doesn't have any of these risks isn't a confined space in legal terms. But the assumption that a space is safe because it looks safe is what kills people. Testing is what proves it.

## What the confined space permit must cover

A confined space permit in UK construction should cover seven areas, at minimum.

**The space itself, location, description and known hazards.** Not a vague reference. The exact location, what's in it, what's been in it, what might still be in it.

**Atmospheric testing results.** Oxygen level (should be 20.9% +/- tight tolerance), flammable gas (should be 0% LEL before entry), toxic gases relevant to the space (CO, H2S, others). Testing must be done before entry, and ideally continuously during entry with a personal monitor.

**Authorised entrants.** Named individuals, not a team. Each entrant should be competent, trained and medically fit for confined space work.

**Standby person (topman).** A person outside the space, in constant communication with entrants, whose sole job is to monitor entry and raise the alarm. This person doesn't enter the space under any circumstances.

**Rescue plan.** Not 'call 999'. An actual plan with named rescuers, equipment on-site, and a practised extraction method. UK law requires this to be in place before entry begins.

**Entry method and equipment.** Tripod and winch, breathing apparatus if required, communication equipment, lighting, tools.

**Time limits.** Entry duration, atmospheric re-test intervals, shift changes.

The permit is the document that proves all of this was considered and all of it was in place. If any of these are missing or vague, the permit hasn't done its job.

## Why rescue arrangements matter more than the entry itself

The grim reality of UK confined space work is that most deaths happen during rescue attempts, not during the original entry. Someone collapses inside the space. A colleague rushes in to help. The same conditions that took the first casualty take the rescuer.

This is why UK guidance is clear: the rescue plan is not optional, and it can't rely on emergency services alone. By the time the fire service arrives with trained confined space rescue capability, it's usually too late for an unconscious casualty in a low-oxygen environment.

A proper rescue plan includes:

- Named rescuers on site, trained and equipped
- Retrieval equipment appropriate to the space (tripod, winch, harness, haul line)
- Breathing apparatus if atmospheric rescue is possible
- Communication equipment that works inside the space
- A practised extraction method for that specific space

Digital confined space permits help here because the rescue plan lives with the permit, not in a folder in the site office. Every entrant, the standby person and the supervisor can see it on their phone. That matters when something goes wrong and seconds count.

## Digital permits for confined space entry

Digital confined space permits aren't about replacing the standby person or the atmospheric monitor. They're about removing the excuses for things being incomplete.

A paper confined space permit typically sits in the site cabin. The standby person at the hatch has a copy. The entrants might have the briefing content but not the full permit. If something changes, a second entry is needed, a gas reading shifts, a rescuer has to be swapped, the paper permit either gets updated everywhere or in one place and drifts out of sync.

A digital permit keeps everyone on the same version. Atmospheric test results can be logged with timestamps. The standby person can see who's inside and when they went in. If the atmospheric monitor alarms, the incident is logged immediately. If entry extends beyond the planned window, the permit flags it.

On the compliance side, a confined space fatality triggers an HSE investigation that digs into every detail. A digital permit system with full timestamps and signatures is the difference between 'we followed our procedures' and 'we can prove we followed our procedures'.

## Common failures in confined space work

Teams that have near-misses in confined space work, and many have more than they report, tend to share the same failings.

**Treating routine entries as lower risk.** A space that's been entered a hundred times without incident is not automatically safe. Conditions change. Biological decay, leaks, adjacent work, weather, the space that was fine yesterday isn't guaranteed fine today.

**Combining entries on a single permit.** One permit per space per entry window. If a team entered at 9am and plans to re-enter at 2pm, that's two permit events with two atmospheric tests, not one permit covering the day.

**Relying on a single atmospheric test.** Conditions inside a confined space change. Continuous monitoring during entry is standard practice. A single pre-entry test isn't enough for any entry lasting more than a few minutes.

**Standby person doubling as something else.** The topman's job is to watch the entry and raise the alarm. If they're also supervising other work, answering the phone or doing paperwork, they're not doing their job. This is a common and dangerous compromise.

**Assuming rescue services will handle an emergency.** They won't get there in time for atmospheric incidents. The rescue plan must be on-site and practised.

## Frequently asked questions

**What are the Confined Spaces Regulations 1997?**
UK regulations that set out employer duties for work in confined spaces. The core duty is to avoid entry if possible, and if entry is necessary, to ensure safe systems of work and emergency arrangements are in place.

**Is a permit to work legally required for confined space entry in the UK?**
The regulations don't specify a permit by name, but they require a safe system of work that typically is documented through a permit. In practice, UK construction sites and HSE inspectors expect a permit for any confined space entry.

**What's the difference between a confined space and a restricted space?**
A confined space has foreseeable hazards (atmosphere, engulfment, fire, heat). A restricted space just has limited access. A tight crawl space in a loft isn't a confined space unless one of the specified hazards is present.

**Can sub-contractors use our confined space permit system?**
They should use whatever system controls the work on your site. If a subbie brings their own paperwork, it needs to align with the principal contractor's permit process, not replace it.

**How often should confined space training be refreshed?**
Every 2-3 years for entrants and standby persons, and whenever the type of confined space work changes. Rescue training should be practised more frequently, at least annually, ideally with live exercises.
