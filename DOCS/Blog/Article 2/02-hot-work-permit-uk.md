Most construction fires that do serious damage aren't caused by something unexpected. They're caused by grinding, welding, cutting or brazing in an area that wasn't prepared, without someone watching after the work stopped. That's why **a hot work permit** exists, and why UK construction insurers increasingly treat it as non-negotiable.

The permit isn't difficult to fill in. Getting it to actually control the risk is the harder part.

Hot work is usually issued under a wider [permit to work app](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk) or system alongside height, confined space and electrical permits, so it's worth checking the whole workflow, not just the hot-work template.

## What counts as hot work on a UK construction site

Hot work is any activity that produces heat, flame, sparks or embers capable of igniting combustible material. On UK construction sites that typically means:

- Welding (arc, MIG, TIG, gas)
- Grinding and disc cutting
- Cutting with oxy-fuel torches
- Bitumen boilers and torch-on roofing
- Soldering and brazing
- Blow torches for paint removal or lead work
- Thermal lances and angle grinders on metalwork

The common factor isn't the tool. It's the potential for ignition in an area that isn't a designated hot work environment. A welding bay in a workshop doesn't need a permit per weld. The same welder operating inside a partially completed building, on a roof, or next to stored materials does.

## What a hot work permit must cover

A proper **hot work permit UK construction teams can rely on** covers six areas:

**Work description and location.** What task, where exactly, using what equipment. Vague entries like "grinding on roof" are a red flag. Specificity is what makes the permit useful to the person doing the fire watch.

**Time window.** Start time, end time, and the fire watch duration after work stops. The permit should expire automatically, an open-ended permit isn't a control.

**Competent person sign-off.** Someone authorised to approve hot work, not just the person doing it. This is where paper permits often go wrong, because the signature on the page doesn't prove the authoriser actually reviewed the area.

**Area preparation checks.** Combustibles removed or covered to minimum 10 metres, drain and cavity checks, flammable liquids moved, dust control if relevant.

**Fire precautions.** Extinguisher type and location, fire blanket if applicable, water supply if needed, isolation of smoke detectors where relevant (and crucially, their re-activation afterwards).

**Fire watch arrangements.** Named fire watcher, duration, communication method. This is the area that gets skipped most on paper permits and is also the area most often involved when a fire does start.

The HSE provides guidance through HSG168 (Fire Safety in Construction) and industry bodies like FPA publish hot work permit templates. The detail matters, because a generic template filled in vaguely is worse than no permit at all, it creates the illusion of control.

## The fire watch, where most permits fail

Here's the pattern. Hot work finishes at 3:30pm. The operative packs up. The supervisor signs the permit closed. Everyone's off site by 4pm. At 5:15pm, a smouldering ember inside wall insulation catches. The building burns overnight.

This happens because **the fire watch period after hot work ends** is the single most-missed step in UK construction hot work. A decent permit requires a minimum fire watch of 60 minutes after the last spark, and many insurers require longer. Some require two hours. Some require the fire watch to include a second check before the site is finally locked up.

Paper permits struggle with this because the close-out signature happens at the end of the work, not at the end of the fire watch. By the time the fire watch period is done, nobody's thinking about the permit. A digital permit can force a separate close-out after the fire watch period, with a timestamp and a photo, so the evidence of the check actually exists.

## Paper vs digital hot work permits

Paper hot work permits work. They've worked for decades. The question isn't whether they function. It's whether they function well enough for a modern UK construction site.

On paper, a hot work permit is a carbon-copy pad in the site cabin. Filled in by hand. Signed by two people. Filed at the end of the day. In theory, retrievable.

In practice:
- The pad isn't always in the cabin when a permit is needed
- The signature of the authoriser sometimes happens after the work starts
- The fire watch close-out rarely gets signed when it should
- Permits get lost, damaged or filed wrongly
- An audit six months later means digging through folders

A digital hot work permit handles the same process but removes those failure points. The form is always accessible on a phone. The authoriser has to sign digitally before the permit goes active. The fire watch close-out is a scheduled reminder. The record is searchable in two seconds.

None of this makes hot work safer by itself. What it does is remove the excuses. The permit lives where the work lives.

## Common mistakes sites make with hot work

Teams that get hot work wrong tend to make the same handful of mistakes.

**Treating it as a generic permit.** A hot work permit isn't a general work permit with the word 'hot' added. The controls are specific to fire risk, and a one-size-fits-all permit template usually misses the area-specific preparation checks.

**Rushing the area preparation.** The 10-metre rule for combustibles isn't a suggestion. If the area can't be cleared, the permit should require physical fire barriers, not verbal assurances.

**Skipping detector isolation documentation.** Sites that isolate smoke detectors to prevent false alarms must document both the isolation and the re-activation. Missing the re-activation step is how buildings end up without working fire detection overnight.

**Letting supervisors issue permits on themselves.** The person doing the work should not be the person authorising it. This basic segregation gets broken regularly on smaller sites, and it undermines the whole permit process.

**Closing the permit before the fire watch ends.** The single most common failing. Close-out is not end-of-work. It's end-of-fire-watch. Platforms that enforce this separation make a real difference.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a hot work permit legally required in the UK?**
There's no single regulation that mandates a hot work permit by name, but UK law (HSWA 1974, Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, CDM 2015, Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005) requires employers to identify and control fire risks. For hot work, a permit is the established means of doing that, and most UK construction insurers require it explicitly.

**How long should the fire watch last after hot work?**
HSE and FPA guidance typically specifies a minimum of 60 minutes after work stops. Many insurers require longer, often 2 hours, sometimes more on higher-risk sites. Check the specific insurance requirements for the project.

**Can the same person issue and carry out hot work?**
No. The authoriser and the operative should be separate people. The authoriser is confirming that the area has been prepared and the precautions are in place. If the same person is doing the work, that check isn't independent.

**Are digital hot work permits accepted by UK insurers?**
Generally yes, provided they capture the same information, signatures and evidence as a paper permit. Some insurers may have specific requirements, worth checking the project insurance policy before rolling out a digital system.

**What's the biggest cause of hot work fires in UK construction?**
Fires that start in concealed areas (wall cavities, void spaces, insulation) after work has finished. The combination of an ember reaching somewhere the operative couldn't see, and the fire watch ending too early, is the most common pattern.
