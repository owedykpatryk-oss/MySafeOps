A two-person building firm fitting a kitchen extension for a homeowner. A sole trader plumber replacing a boiler for a family business. An electrician rewiring a commercial unit on a Saturday morning.

All three are contractors under the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015. All three have specific legal duties. And the vast majority of small UK contractors working today have never read the regulations, don't have a Construction Phase Plan, and assume CDM applies only to "proper" construction sites with scaffolding and site offices.

This is the guide they don't get handed at trade shows. Plain English. What **CDM 2015** actually requires if you're a small contractor, sole trader, or 2-5 person firm. What to do. What to skip. Where the real liability sits.

## Who counts as a "small contractor" under CDM 2015

The regulations don't use the phrase "small contractor". They use one word: **contractor**. And it covers almost everyone doing physical construction work.

From the legal definition: a contractor is anyone who directly employs or engages construction workers, or manages construction work. That includes:

- Sole traders (self-employed tradespeople)
- Two-person partnerships
- Small limited companies doing construction, refurbishment, or maintenance
- Plumbers, electricians, roofers, joiners, groundworkers, decorators
- Kitchen fitters, bathroom installers, boiler installers
- Anyone doing extensions, loft conversions, garden rooms
- Shop fitters, commercial maintenance firms

Crucially, the regulations apply whether the workers under your control are **employees, self-employed, or agency workers**. Bringing in a mate for the day to help shift materials? That makes you a contractor managing construction work.

There's no "small project" exemption. The 2007 regulations had minor work exemptions that got removed in the 2015 revision. A contractor doing a £2,000 bathroom fit-out has the same duties as a contractor doing a £2 million office refurbishment. The scale of effort is different. The legal duties are the same.

## The three things CDM 2015 changed that still catch small firms out

The 2015 revision made three changes that small UK contractors still trip over in 2026.

**Change 1: The "CDM coordinator" role was abolished.**
Pre-2015, many small projects had a CDM coordinator handling the paperwork on behalf of the client. That role no longer exists. It was replaced by the **Principal Designer** role, which sits with whoever prepares or modifies design during the pre-construction phase. On a small domestic job with no architect, the contractor often ends up carrying design duties as well as contractor duties.

**Change 2: Domestic clients now count.**
Before 2015, domestic clients (homeowners paying for work on their own house) were largely exempt. Now, CDM applies to domestic projects. The twist: if the homeowner fails to appoint a Principal Designer or Principal Contractor, those duties **automatically transfer to the contractor doing the work**. A plumber replacing a boiler for a homeowner who "doesn't know what a CDM plan is" is legally responsible for both the contractor duties and the Principal Contractor duties.

**Change 3: The "suitable and sufficient" risk assessment test got harder.**
Risk assessments under CDM have to consider not just direct workers but anyone who might be affected, including members of the public, other trades, visitors, and the client. A [RAMS](https://mysafeops.com/blog/how-to-write-a-rams-uk) focused only on the operatives doing the work is incomplete under the 2015 test.

## Your duties as a contractor (2-person firm or sole trader)

If you are any kind of contractor under CDM 2015, you have these duties. They apply regardless of whether you're the principal contractor or a subcontractor.

**1. Plan, manage and monitor your own work.**
Don't just turn up and start. Think through hazards, control measures, sequence of work, access and materials. For all but the smallest tasks, this gets written down as a Risk Assessment and Method Statement (RAMS).

**2. Ensure workers have the right skills, knowledge and experience.**
Workers you use, whether employed, self-employed or agency, must be competent for the work. Documented evidence helps: CSCS cards, trade qualifications, training records, specific certifications (like SSSTS/SMSTS, PASMA, IPAF, NEBOSH, CITB cards).

**3. Provide site-specific induction to workers under your control.**
Unless the principal contractor has already covered this. Even on small jobs, new workers need to know the hazards, welfare arrangements, emergency procedures and reporting routes. Documented with a signature. Not optional.

**4. Provide appropriate supervision, information and instructions.**
A trained operative left alone on a site without supervision is a CDM failing even if nothing goes wrong. Supervision doesn't mean standing over them. It means being reachable, checking progress, verifying method.

**5. Prevent unauthorised access.**
For small jobs this might be a simple physical barrier and signage. For domestic work, it means your tools and materials aren't left accessible to children, pets or unrelated visitors.

**6. Provide suitable welfare facilities.**
Even a 2-person job needs basic welfare. Toilet access, handwashing, drinking water, somewhere to sit during breaks. On a small domestic job, this is usually agreed with the homeowner. On a small commercial job, it's the contractor's responsibility.

**7. Cooperate with other duty holders.**
If you're a subcontractor on a larger project, you cooperate with the Principal Contractor. If you're the only contractor on a small job, you cooperate with the client (domestic or commercial). Cooperation means sharing information, coordinating work, avoiding conflicts.

## Your extra duties as principal contractor (single-contractor jobs)

If you're the **only contractor** on a project, you are also the Principal Contractor by default. This triggers additional duties.

**1. Prepare a Construction Phase Plan (CPP) before starting site.**
This is the big one that catches small contractors out. Even a 2-day bathroom fit-out needs a CPP if you're the only contractor. It can be short (2-4 pages for a simple job), but it has to exist.

**2. Plan, manage and monitor the construction phase for health and safety.**
You're no longer just managing your own work. You're responsible for how health and safety is managed across the whole phase.

**3. Consult and engage with workers.**
On a 2-person job this is simpler than on a 50-person site, but the duty still exists. Workers have a right to raise concerns and be informed of risks.

**4. Liaise with the client.**
Keep the client informed. If they're a domestic client who doesn't understand CDM, explain what you're doing and why. Document the conversations.

**5. Coordinate contractors.**
If you're the Principal Contractor and subcontractors get added later, you coordinate their work with yours.

**6. Ensure welfare and worker information is in place.**
Same as the contractor duties above, but with responsibility for everyone, not just your own workers.

**On projects involving more than one contractor,** the client appoints a Principal Contractor (usually the main builder). You only become the Principal Contractor if you're the only one, or if the client fails to make an appointment.

## What a Construction Phase Plan actually has to contain

HSE guidance L153 is the definitive reference, but for small contractors the practical content of a CPP breaks down into seven sections.

**Section 1: Project description and key dates**
- Client name and address
- Site location (if different)
- Project summary (what's being done)
- Start and end dates
- Key milestones

**Section 2: Management arrangements**
- Who's in charge on site (named person)
- Reporting lines
- Communication methods
- How site meetings or briefings will run

**Section 3: Site rules and welfare**
- Working hours
- PPE requirements
- Welfare arrangements (toilet, handwash, break area)
- Access control
- Smoking, parking, deliveries

**Section 4: Key health and safety risks and how they'll be controlled**
- List the real hazards of the specific work
- For each hazard, the control measure
- Reference any [permits](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk) required (hot work, confined space, height)
- Reference any specialist regulations (Work at Height 2005, Electricity at Work 1989, [COSHH 2002](https://mysafeops.com/blog/coshh-register-software-uk))

**Section 5: Emergency procedures**
- First aid arrangements
- Nearest A&E
- Accident and incident reporting procedure
- Fire safety and evacuation

**Section 6: Site induction and competency**
- How workers will be inducted
- What competency evidence is required
- Recording of induction attendance

**Section 7: Monitoring and review**
- How H&S performance will be checked
- When the CPP will be reviewed
- Who's responsible for updates

A CPP for a 2-day domestic bathroom fit-out might be 3-4 pages. For a 6-month commercial refurbishment it might be 20-30 pages. The content scales to the project.

## The domestic client trap

This is where small UK contractors get caught most often. Here's the scenario.

A homeowner asks you to rewire their house. They don't mention CDM, because they've never heard of it. You quote, agree a price, start work. Legally, under CDM 2015, the homeowner is the client. Clients have duties. But domestic clients (as the regulations recognise) aren't typically aware of those duties.

**What the regulations actually say:**
- Domestic clients still have CDM duties, but HSE recognises these are usually light-touch
- If the domestic client fails to appoint a Principal Designer, those duties transfer to the designer (if there is one) or to the contractor
- If the domestic client fails to appoint a Principal Contractor and there's more than one contractor involved, those duties transfer to the contractor in control of the construction phase

In practice, on most small domestic jobs:
- The homeowner doesn't formally appoint anyone
- The contractor becomes Principal Contractor by default
- The Principal Contractor duties (including CPP) sit with the contractor

Many sole traders operate without knowing this. Most jobs go fine. But when something goes wrong, HSE investigation starts with the paperwork. No CPP on a domestic job where you were sole contractor is a prosecutable CDM failing.

## Common CDM failures that get small contractors prosecuted

From published HSE enforcement cases over 2020-2026, the patterns for small contractor prosecutions are clear.

**Fatality on a domestic extension.**
Sole trader roofer falls from a ladder while fitting new fascia board. HSE investigation finds no risk assessment, no method statement, no site-specific induction for the apprentice who was helping. Prosecution under Work at Height Regulations 2005 and CDM 2015 Regulation 8 and Regulation 13. Fine typically £20,000-£80,000, plus costs.

**Serious injury from trench collapse.**
Two-person drainage contractor working on a domestic project. No CPP. Trench collapsed on apprentice. Survived but serious injury. Prosecution for failure to plan, manage and monitor under CDM Regulation 13.

**Child injured on unfenced domestic site.**
Small building firm leaves domestic extension site overnight with tools and materials accessible. Child climbs in, cuts themselves on a circular saw. CDM prosecution for failure to prevent unauthorised access under Regulation 13, plus civil claim from parents.

**Electrical injury on commercial refurbishment.**
Small M&E contractor didn't verify isolation before working on switchgear. Operative survived but serious burns. CDM and Electricity at Work Regulations 1989 prosecution. Fine £45,000.

The pattern in all four: the work wasn't planned properly, the paperwork wasn't in place, and when something went wrong the contractor couldn't demonstrate compliance. The cost of the paperwork would have been 1-3 hours of time. The cost of not having it was career-ending for the contractors involved.

## Free Construction Phase Plan template structure

Here's the structure of a CPP you can use as a small contractor. Copy the sections, fill in the content, keep the language plain. This is a starting structure, not a replacement for proper thought about the specific job.

### Section A: Cover sheet
- Project name
- Client name
- Site address
- Principal Contractor (your firm)
- Date prepared
- Revision number
- Review due date

### Section B: Project description
- What the project involves (3-5 sentences)
- Start date
- Expected duration
- Key phases if phased

### Section C: Roles and responsibilities
- Named Principal Contractor representative on site
- Reporting lines
- Contact numbers for key personnel

### Section D: Site rules
- Working hours
- Access and parking
- Minimum PPE
- Smoking, alcohol, drugs policy
- Welfare facilities

### Section E: Key hazards and controls
Table format:
- Hazard description
- Who is affected
- Control measures
- Reference to RAMS / permit / specific regulation

### Section F: Emergency arrangements
- First aid provision
- Nearest hospital
- Fire safety
- Accident reporting
- Emergency contacts

### Section G: Induction and competence
- Who inducts new arrivals
- Documentation required
- Competency evidence expected

### Section H: Communication and coordination
- How information flows between contractor, client, others
- Regular briefing arrangements
- Method of raising concerns

### Section I: Monitoring and review
- Checks during the construction phase
- When the CPP gets reviewed
- Who approves updates

### Section J: Sign-offs
- Principal Contractor (you) sign and date
- Optional client acknowledgement
- Review date

For a small job this template fills out to 3-5 pages. For a bigger job it expands to whatever it needs to be. The key is that it exists, is relevant to the actual work, and is reviewed when circumstances change.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need a CPP for every small job?**
If you're the only contractor, yes. Even a 2-day job. CDM 2015 Regulation 15 requires a CPP before the construction phase starts when there's only one contractor. The CPP can be short, but it needs to exist.

**What counts as "construction work" under CDM?**
Broad definition: building, altering, converting, fitting out, commissioning, renovating, repairing, upkeep, redecoration, other maintenance, demolition, dismantling, site clearance, services and infrastructure work. If what you're doing touches a structure or its fabric, it's probably construction under CDM.

**Does CDM apply to emergency callouts?**
Yes. There's no emergency exemption. If you're doing construction work in response to an urgent call, CDM still applies. Practical approach: have a generic CPP for common emergency work types that can be rapidly site-specific.

**What's the minimum training a small contractor needs?**
CDM doesn't specify training, but requires competence. Common practical evidence: CSCS card at appropriate level, SSSTS for supervisors, SMSTS for site managers, IOSH Managing Safely or NEBOSH for H&S responsibilities. Trade-specific qualifications for the work being done. CPCS for plant, IPAF for MEWPs, PASMA for mobile towers.

**Can I use a generic CPP template?**
A template is a starting point, not a deliverable. A generic CPP with no site-specific content is worse than no CPP, because it shows you produced a document but didn't think about the actual job. Always customise.

**Who checks if my CDM paperwork is in order?**
HSE can inspect at any time, with or without notice. Local authority environmental health officers have parallel powers in non-HSE premises. Insurance companies may ask for evidence following an incident. Clients increasingly request CDM paperwork as part of contract award.

**Is there a CDM exemption for very small jobs?**
No. The 2015 revision removed almost all minor work exemptions. Even a single-day repair job has CDM duties if it counts as construction work.

**What's the difference between CDM and Building Safety Act 2022?**
CDM 2015 covers all construction work and focuses on managing health and safety during design and construction. The Building Safety Act 2022 covers higher-risk buildings (mostly residential over 18m or 7+ storeys) and introduces additional duties during design and construction. For most small contractors, CDM is the relevant framework. Building Safety Act applies only to specific building types.

## CDM paperwork isn't optional, but it doesn't have to be a job

The common misconception among small UK contractors is that CDM compliance is either unnecessary or impossibly bureaucratic. Neither is true. It's legally mandatory, but for most small jobs it can be handled with a 3-4 page CPP, proper RAMS for the specific work, and documented worker induction. Total prep time: 2-4 hours for a typical small job. The liability of not doing it can end a business.
