### Asset 3: LinkedIn Long-Form Essay

```
In 2013, RIDDOR dropped the list of reportable occupational diseases from 47 to six.

At the time, it was framed as cutting unnecessary admin burden. A simpler list, easier reporting, less paperwork for employers. The logic made sense on paper.

Thirteen years later, HSE has come back to ask: did we cut too much?

The consultation opened last week. It proposes restoring nine diseases to the list and adding four new ones, taking the total from 6 to 19. The ones coming back are the conditions construction workers have been quietly getting for decades without formal reporting visibility:

• Asbestosis
• Silicosis and other pneumoconiosis
• Decompression illness (tunnelling, diving)
• Significant hearing loss
• Respiratory and skin beryllium disease
• Various other conditions from the pre-2013 list

If you've worked on UK construction sites through the 1990s and 2000s, you know people who have these conditions. The 2013 reform didn't make them go away. It just took them out of the official record.

The rest of the consultation is about dangerous occurrences, and it hits construction hard. Plant overturning becomes reportable without injury. Tunnelling incidents over 1.2m come into scope. Falling objects outside lifting operations. Trench collapses and temporary works failures are called out explicitly.

All of this is on the table until 30 June 2026.

Here's the part most firms miss: consultations are where regulation actually gets shaped. Not in the final publication. If the industry doesn't respond, the rules get finalised with whatever HSE thinks is reasonable, which isn't always what works on a live site.

Site managers, H&S leads, operations directors, subcontractors: this is the consultation you want to respond to. Your site experience matters more than a legal team's opinion from an office.

Full breakdown of what's proposed and how to submit a response here: https://mysafeops.com/blog/riddor-changes-2026

#UKConstruction #HSE #OccupationalHealth #RIDDOR
```

### Asset 7: Quora Answer

**Question target:** "What are the upcoming RIDDOR changes in 2026?"

**Answer:**
```
HSE opened a consultation on RIDDOR on 7 April 2026 that, if adopted, would be the biggest reform since 2013. Four main areas:

**1. Dangerous occurrences (hits construction hard)**
Plant overturning becomes reportable without injury (excavators, mobile plant, drill rigs, piling rigs). Tunnelling incidents over 1.2m diameter come into scope. Falling objects outside lifting operations, where they could cause death, become reportable. Trench collapse and temporary works failure explicitly included.

**2. Occupational disease list expands from 6 to 19**
Nine conditions come back (dropped in 2013): asbestosis, silicosis and other pneumoconiosis, decompression illness, significant hearing loss, beryllium disease variants. Four new ones added: noise-induced hearing loss formalised, bronchiolitis obliterans, occupational allergic rhinitis, occupational contact urticaria.

**3. Diagnosis authority broadens**
Currently only GMC-registered doctors can diagnose a reportable disease. Proposed: extend to other registered health practitioners (specialist occupational health nurses, physiotherapists, etc.).

**4. Online reporting form gets simplified**
To tackle chronic under-reporting and over-reporting on busy sites.

**Consultation closes 30 June 2026.** Responses via HSE online survey, email or post.

Full breakdown of what's changing and how to respond: https://mysafeops.com/blog/riddor-changes-2026

Source: HSE Media Centre, 7 April 2026. HSE consultation page: https://consultations.hse.gov.uk/hse/proposals-riddor-2013/
```
