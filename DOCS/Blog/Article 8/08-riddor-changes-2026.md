On 7 April 2026, the Health and Safety Executive opened a consultation that, if adopted in full, will reshape how UK construction firms report incidents and occupational ill health. It's the first major review of **RIDDOR** since the 2013 amendments, and the proposals land squarely on construction activity. Tunnelling, plant overturning, falling objects, and a list of occupational diseases that would roughly treble.

If you're a principal contractor, contractor, self-employed tradesperson or anyone in control of a UK construction site, this consultation affects you. The response window closes on **30 June 2026**, and the proposals deserve more than a skim.

Platforms like [MySafeOps](https://mysafeops.com) help construction firms keep incident records structured and audit-ready regardless of how RIDDOR evolves, but the changes themselves are what matter first. Here's what's on the table.

## Why the RIDDOR changes matter for UK construction

RIDDOR (the Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013) is the legal framework that tells UK employers what workplace incidents they must report to HSE. For construction, it covers fatalities, specified injuries, over-7-day incapacitation, dangerous occurrences, and a short list of occupational diseases.

The current regulations have two recognised problems. They under-capture genuinely serious incidents on modern construction sites, particularly around plant safety and ill health. And the online reporting form creates friction that produces both under-reporting (people don't bother) and over-reporting (people report non-reportable events to be safe). HSE wants to fix both.

What makes this consultation specifically relevant to construction is that the proposed changes to dangerous occurrences target activities common on UK sites: excavator use, piling, tunnelling, demolition. The proposed expansion of occupational diseases brings back conditions that construction workers have been exposed to for decades but which vanished from the reporting list in 2013. Asbestos-related diseases. Silicosis. Hand-arm vibration. Conditions that don't show up for years but trace back to specific construction tasks.

In short: if the proposals pass, construction firms will be reporting more, and will need to track occupational health over longer timeframes than the current regulations anticipate.

## What's changing: dangerous occurrences

This is the part of the consultation that will hit UK construction sites hardest. HSE is proposing four specific changes to what counts as a reportable dangerous occurrence.

**Plant overturning becomes reportable.** The consultation proposes mandatory reporting of overturning incidents involving excavators, mobile plant, drill rigs and piling rigs, where the overturning could cause a specified injury or death to a person. At present, these incidents only get logged with HSE if they result in actual injury. Under the proposed rules, a near-miss involving a toppled excavator on a busy site would trigger a RIDDOR report.

For most UK civils contractors, this is a significant administrative shift. Plant overturning incidents are not rare, and near-misses (where nobody is hurt but the consequences could have been severe) happen often enough that reporting them will be a recurring task, not an exception.

**Tunnelling incidents over 1.2m diameter come into scope.** Tunnelling has always been a high-risk activity but RIDDOR has treated it inconsistently. The proposed rules bring tunnelling firmly into the dangerous occurrence list, with incidents in tunnels over 1.2 metres in diameter becoming reportable. This is most relevant to civils firms working on major infrastructure projects, HS2-style works, drainage and utility tunnelling, and mining-adjacent construction.

**Falling objects outside lifting operations become reportable.** Currently, falling object incidents are mostly reportable only when associated with failed lifting equipment. The proposed change widens this significantly: any unintentional drop from a structure under construction or demolition that could cause death would be reportable. HSE has acknowledged this provision could generate a high volume of reports and has proposed the "could cause the death of a person" qualifier to limit reporting to serious events.

In practice, this means a bolt dropping from scaffolding onto a pedestrian pathway below is potentially reportable even if nobody is hurt. How this plays out on site depends heavily on the final wording and guidance HSE issues after the consultation closes.

**Structural collapse clarified to include roofs, ceilings, temporary works and trench collapses.** The current RIDDOR wording around collapse is broad but has been interpreted inconsistently. The proposed amendment makes it explicit: trench collapses in excavations, collapse of temporary works (falsework, scaffold), roof or ceiling collapse during refurbishment, and similar failures all fall within the dangerous occurrence definition.

For excavation work, trench supports and temporary works managed through your wider [permit to work process](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk), this changes the stakes. A trench collapse without injury currently sits in a grey area. Under the proposed rules, it's unambiguously reportable.

## What's changing: reportable occupational diseases

The bigger long-term change is to the list of reportable occupational diseases. The current RIDDOR list has six conditions, down from 47 before the 2013 reforms. HSE is proposing to expand the list to **19 conditions**, reintroducing nine previously removed and adding new ones.

**Nine diseases proposed for reintroduction:**

1. Asbestosis
2. Pneumoconiosis (including silicosis)
3. Respiratory beryllium disease
4. Skin beryllium disease
5. Decompression illness (linked to tunnelling and diving)
6. Significant hearing loss (noise-induced)
7. Further asbestos-related conditions
8. Further pneumoconiosis variants
9. Other historical conditions from the pre-2013 list

**New diseases proposed for addition:**

- Noise-induced hearing loss (formalised)
- Bronchiolitis obliterans
- Occupational allergic rhinitis
- Occupational contact urticaria

For UK construction, the implications are substantial. Asbestos-related diseases are common in refurbishment and demolition work. Silicosis affects concrete cutters, stone masons, tunnellers and anyone exposed to respirable crystalline silica without adequate RPE. Noise-induced hearing loss is endemic on sites with prolonged exposure to power tools, plant and demolition activity.

These are not rare conditions among construction workers, particularly older workers. The 2013 reforms reduced visibility of this ill health by shortening the reportable list. Reintroducing these conditions means construction firms will need systems in place to track long-latency occupational health, not just immediate injury.

## Who can diagnose an occupational disease

Currently, a reportable occupational disease under RIDDOR must be diagnosed by a doctor registered with (and holding a licence to practise with) the General Medical Council. This restriction has created bottlenecks, because many occupational health services are delivered by specialist nurses, physiotherapists and occupational health advisors who aren't GMC-registered doctors.

The consultation proposes extending diagnostic authority to "other registered health practitioners". The precise list isn't finalised and is one of the questions the consultation asks stakeholders to respond to. Likely candidates include specialist occupational health nurses, physiotherapists working in occupational health, audiologists for hearing loss assessments, and dermatologists who aren't doctors (specialist nurses).

For construction firms using external occupational health providers, this change would simplify reporting where the provider isn't a GMC-registered doctor. It also means more diagnoses will qualify for RIDDOR reporting, which ties back to the expanded disease list.

## The online form overhaul

Alongside the legislative changes, HSE is proposing non-legislative improvements to the RIDDOR reporting process itself. The main target is the online form.

HSE has acknowledged two persistent issues with the current form:

- **Under-reporting**, where the form is confusing or time-consuming enough that responsible persons don't submit reports they legally should
- **Over-reporting**, where the form encourages precautionary reporting of events that aren't actually reportable, cluttering HSE's dataset

The consultation asks stakeholders how the form should be simplified. This is a genuinely useful opportunity for UK construction firms to shape the tool they'll be using regularly. If your site managers and H&S leads have specific frustrations with the current form (and most do), this is the moment to put them in writing.

## What construction firms should do before 30 June

Practical steps over the next 10 weeks.

**1. Read the consultation document.** It's available directly on the HSE consultations site: https://consultations.hse.gov.uk/hse/proposals-riddor-2013/. The full document is detailed but focused, and most construction firms can get through the relevant sections in 45-60 minutes.

**2. Identify the proposals that affect your operations.** A tunnelling contractor needs to focus on dangerous occurrence changes. A demolition firm needs to focus on asbestos-related disease reporting. A groundworks contractor needs to focus on plant overturning and trench collapse. The consultation covers a lot; pick the parts that matter to your work.

**3. Consult your site teams.** The best responses aren't from the HSE compliance desk alone. Site managers, H&S leads, foremen and occupational health providers all have views on what works and what doesn't in current RIDDOR practice. Gather those views before drafting a response.

**4. Submit a response.** Three options:
- Online survey (easiest): via the HSE consultation page
- Email: download the Word version and email to RIDDORconsultation2026@hse.gov.uk
- Post: to RIDDOR Consultation, Health and Safety Executive, Building 2.2, Redgrave Court, Merton Road, Bootle, Merseyside, L20 7HS

**5. Plan for the changes.** Regardless of whether you submit a response, the proposals will shape the next iteration of RIDDOR. Start thinking now about how your [incident and near-miss reporting](https://mysafeops.com/blog/free-safety-app-construction-workers) will handle the expanded scope. If you're on paper forms, the incoming changes make digital tracking materially more valuable.

## What happens after 30 June

HSE will review responses, potentially refine the proposals, and publish a summary. Legislative changes require wider Government agreement and typically take 6-18 months to come into force after consultation close. Non-legislative changes (form simplification) can happen faster.

Based on precedent, the earliest any amended RIDDOR regulations would come into force is likely early 2027, though partial changes could happen sooner. Construction firms have time to prepare, but the time to influence the shape of the rules is now, not later.

## Frequently asked questions

**When does the RIDDOR consultation close?**
The consultation closes on 30 June 2026. Responses received after that date cannot be guaranteed to influence HSE's decision.

**Do I have to respond to the consultation?**
Responding is not mandatory. But HSE specifically encourages duty holders, self-employed workers and those in control of work premises to respond, because the changes affect their operations directly. Not responding means your views don't count towards the final proposals.

**Will the changes apply to construction specifically, or all industries?**
RIDDOR applies across all UK industries, but several of the proposed changes (plant overturning, tunnelling, trench collapse) are especially relevant to construction. Other sectors (manufacturing, healthcare) have their own focus areas in the consultation.

**How many new RIDDOR reports will our site need to submit if the changes pass?**
Impossible to predict precisely, but the expansion of dangerous occurrence categories and the tripled disease list will increase reporting volume for most UK construction firms. Firms already tracking near-misses and occupational health well will see the smallest operational change.

**Where do I find the full RIDDOR consultation document?**
Directly at https://consultations.hse.gov.uk/hse/proposals-riddor-2013/. The page includes the consultation document, supporting information and the online response survey.

**Can I respond on behalf of a trade association or industry body?**
Yes. HSE welcomes responses from individual firms, trade associations, professional bodies, and representative organisations. Industry-wide responses often carry significant weight in consultations.

The RIDDOR changes proposed for 2026 aren't minor housekeeping. The consultation is the last chance for the industry to shape the rules before HSE finalises its proposals. Construction firms that respond carefully, based on real site experience, will get better-designed rules than those that stay silent.
