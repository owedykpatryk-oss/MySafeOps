Breaking ground looks straightforward until a gas main, 11 kV cable or fibre trunk is where the drawing said it wasn't. HSE's guidance on avoiding danger from underground services (HSG47) is clear: assume services exist until proved otherwise. On busy UK civils and building sites, an **excavation permit UK** teams actually use is the difference between that assumption being real on the ground or just a line in the pre-start meeting.

Excavation work often runs alongside [site induction](https://mysafeops.com/blog/site-induction-software-uk) and the wider [permit to work app](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk) family — especially when digs interact with [confined space entry](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk) in deep chambers.

## Why excavation permits matter on UK sites

An excavation permit is not a replacement for utility surveys or CAT scans. It is the authorisation layer that confirms:

- Utility drawings and PAS 128 survey level are available and understood
- Detection equipment is calibrated and used by a competent person
- The dig method matches the service risk (hand dig zones, vacuum excavation, mechanical restrictions)
- Somebody competent has approved the box before machines start

Principal contractors use permits to stop "quick holes" appearing without a named supervisor, without service marks, and without a plan for what happens when the soil behaves differently than the desk study suggested.

## What must be on the permit

**Location and extent.** Drawing reference, GPS or grid, maximum depth, length and width.

**Buried services.** Known electric, gas, water, telecom, fibre; mark-up photo or sketch; responsible utility company contacts if live services remain.

**Detection record.** Cat & Genny scan log, genny mode used, areas rescanned after first spoil removal.

**Method.** Mechanical allowance, hand-dig buffer distances, slurry or vacuum if specified, exclusion zone for spoil and plant.

**Shoring or battering.** Tabbed box, trench sheets, engineered design reference if depth exceeds generic rules.

**Competence.** Named banksman, machine operator, person authorised to stop the dig.

**Adjacent assets.** Foundations, retaining walls, roads, live carriageways — vibration and undermining controls.

**Emergency.** Utility strike procedure, hospital route, isolation contacts on the permit face.

## Utility detection and safe dig methods

The permit should fail closed if:

- Drawings are missing for the dig corridor
- Marks on the ground don't match the latest survey
- Trial pits haven't exposed known crossings where required
- Hand-dig zones around marked services are too narrow for the bucket size in use

Safe dig isn't only hand digging. It's matching the tool to the confirmed service state. A permit extension should be required when the trench deepens or moves laterally — new ground, new scan.

## Depth, shoring and adjacent structures

When depth passes typical benching limits or ground water appears, the permit needs a engineering check or temporary works design reference. "We'll see how it looks" is how collapses and nearby settlement start.

Record daily inspections for open trenches, especially after rain. A permit that's open three days without a condition review is a common audit finding.

## Handback and reinstatement

Close-out should confirm:

- Services exposed are supported and notified if damaged
- Temporary covers or fences are in place
- Backfill and compaction standard is named
- As-built note filed if services were found off-line

Digital handback with photos timestamps the site condition when the permit closed — useful for highway authorities and principal contractor records.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is an excavation permit required by law in the UK?**
CDM and HSG47 require planning and safe systems for ground disturbance. A permit is standard UK practice to demonstrate controls before dig.

**Who can authorise an excavation permit?**
A competent supervisor — often the agent, ganger or temporary works coordinator interface — who understands utilities and the chosen dig method.

**How close can I mechanically dig to a marked electric cable?**
Follow HSG47 and your utility owner's rules; hand dig and expose before mechanical plant gets close. Exact distances depend on voltage and method — never guess from memory.

**What if we find an unmarked service?**
Stop work, isolate if safe to do so, notify utilities and the principal contractor, update drawings, rescan before restart. Extend or reissue the permit.

**Does a permit replace a CAT scan?**
No. The permit records that scans and controls happened; it doesn't substitute for detection.
