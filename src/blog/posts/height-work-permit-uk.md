It's 07:20 on a Tuesday. The MEWP is booked, the cladding crew are waiting, and the wind gauge on the supervisor's phone says 18 mph gusting higher. The RAMS says work can go ahead below 20 mph. Nobody has walked the deck yet to check whether yesterday's guardrails are still in place after the night shift stripped formwork two bays along.

That gap between what the paperwork says and what the edge actually looks like is why UK sites use a **height work permit**. The Work at Height Regulations 2005 (WAHR) do not name "permit" in the law. They require planning, competence and suitable equipment. On construction sites, a signed height permit is how principal contractors and HSE inspectors usually expect you to prove those controls were applied before someone climbs.

Height work sits in the same family as [hot work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk) and [confined space](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk) inside a proper [permit to work system](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk). The hazard here is gravity, not fire or bad air.

## When you need a height work permit on site

Issue a permit when the task cannot be done safely from the ground and the remaining controls need a named authorisation before access. Typical triggers:

- Roof work, including fragile or unknown coverings
- Leading-edge formwork, decking or cladding
- MEWP operation in shared areas or near live lifts
- Ladder or tower work that is not a quick, low-risk task
- Steel erection, edge protection installation or removal
- Work over water, plant or live traffic (often combined with other permits)

If edge protection, nets or a MEWP basket already make the task low risk and the RAMS is signed, some firms treat that as RAMS-only. Where trades overlap, weather can change the method, or the edge condition is uncertain, a permit adds a second gate that takes two minutes on a phone and can stop a fall.

## RAMS vs height permit

| | RAMS | Height work permit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **What it covers** | How the job should be done in general | This task, this place, this shift |
| **Who uses it** | Everyone on the method | Named operatives and supervisor for that access |
| **When it changes** | When the method changes | When weather, edge state or equipment changes |
| **Audit question** | "Was there a safe system?" | "Who authorised access on that morning?" |

A RAMS without a permit can still be compliant for repetitive, low-risk work. A permit without a RAMS is paperwork with no method behind it.

## What the permit must record

A usable height permit is more than "approved to work at height". Minimum fields:

**Location and task.** Grid line, elevation, what is being installed or repaired.

**Method and equipment.** MEWP type and ground conditions, scaffold tag reference, ladder class and tie pattern, fall-arrest lanyard type if PFPE is in use.

**Exclusion and edge protection.** Who controls the drop zone below, state of guardrails and toe boards, netting where specified.

**Competence.** Named operatives, IPAF card for MEWP, CISRS for scaffolders where relevant. Fall-arrest competency is not assumed from induction alone.

**Weather limits.** Wind cut-off for MEWP and crane-suspended platforms, rain affecting fragile roofs or slippery decks.

**Emergency and rescue.** Suspension trauma plan if PFPE is used, ground contact for MEWP emergency lowering, first aider location.

**Duration and handback.** Start time, extension rules, close-out confirming materials secured and holes covered.

Digital permits help at shift handover: the incoming supervisor sees live permit state instead of hunting a clipboard in the cabin.

## MEWP, scaffolding and fragile surfaces

**MEWP permits** should record ground bearing, outriggers deployed, exclusion zone, operator card, and whether the platform shares airspace with crane lifts or overhead power. A MEWP is not a substitute for edge protection on a roof; it is a different control set.

**Scaffold access** should reference the current inspection tag and handover note. A height permit does not replace the [scaffold inspection register](https://mysafeops.com/blog/scaffold-inspection-checklist-uk); it confirms the team uses tagged scaff only.

**Fragile roofs** need explicit surface identification (asbestos cement, liner panels, rooflights), crawl boards or staging, and a no-walk zone marked on the permit sketch where possible. HSE's fragile surfaces guidance (HSG33) is still the reference many principal contractors cite in permit briefings.

## Rescue and suspension trauma

If the method relies on fall-arrest lanyards, the permit must name how a suspended operative will be recovered inside the suspension trauma window. "Call 999" is not a plan. Rescue kit location, trained rescuers and a practised drill belong on the permit or a linked rescue document.

On one housing block job in Manchester, the permit was signed but the rescue kit was still in the stores container, locked, keys with a manager who had left site. That is the kind of detail a paper tick-box misses and a photo upload on close-out catches.

## Common failures HSE sees

- **Permit signed but edge protection not installed:** paperwork theatre.
- **MEWP on soft ground without mats:** outrigger load spread missing from the brief.
- **Roof work after rain without re-assessing fragility:** conditions changed; nobody re-authorised.
- **Shared permits across days:** each shift or material change needs fresh authorisation when the method changes.
- **Fall-arrest selected without anchor assessment:** lanyard on, nowhere safe to tie off.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are work at height permits a legal requirement in the UK?**
WAHR requires planning, competence and suitable equipment. A permit is the usual way UK construction sites show those controls were applied for high-risk height tasks.

**What's the difference between a RAMS and a height work permit?**
RAMS describes how the job should be done. A permit authorises a specific task, place and time when height risk needs an extra control gate.

**Do I need a permit for a short ladder job?**
Low-risk, short-duration ladder use may sit in RAMS and toolbox talk alone. If the task is non-routine, above 2 m with no other controls, or in a busy area, use a permit.

**Who should sign a height work permit?**
A competent supervisor who understands the method (often the site manager, appointed person for scaffolding interfaces, or MEWP supervisor). Authorisation must not be a box-tick from someone off site.

**How does digital help with WAHR compliance?**
Timestamped issue, extension and close-out, photos of edge protection, and worker acknowledgements give audit evidence if HSE or a principal contractor asks what was in place on the day.
