The Lifting Operations and Lifting Equipment Regulations 1998 (LOLER) require lifting equipment to be strong enough, stable enough, and examined on schedule, and lifting operations to be **properly planned** by a competent person. A **lifting operations permit UK** supervisors actually use is the bridge between the lift plan in the folder and the load moving over live ground.

## LOLER and the permit on construction sites

LOLER doesn't name "permits", but it does require:

- Lifting equipment suitable for the task and marked with Safe Working Load
- Thorough examination at statutory intervals and after exceptional circumstances
- Lifting operations planned and supervised by competent people

The permit is how principal contractors ensure a specific lift on a specific day matches an approved method. It should not be issued if the crane exam is out of date or the lift plan doesn't cover the actual radius and load.

## What a lifting operations permit must include

**Lift description.** Load weight and centre of gravity, pick and set points, maximum radius, height, slew restrictions.

**Equipment.** Crane type, registration, SWL at configured radius, rigging gear (slings, shackles) with ID and WLL.

**Examination status.** Reference to current thorough examination report or LOLER cert. **Fail closed if expired**.

**Lift plan reference.** Simple lifts may reference standard RAMS; complex lifts need a named plan and drawing.

**Exclusion zone.** Barriers, banksman positions, no-go areas under the load path.

**Weather limits.** Wind speed cut-off per manufacturer and BS EN 13000 guidance for cranes.

**Personnel.** Appointed person (AP), crane operator, slinger/signaller: names and competence cards.

**Communication.** Radio channel, hand signals, point of contact if the lift pauses.

**Emergency.** Suspend procedure, outrigger failure, contact with overhead lines. Do not lift until this is written down.

## Routine vs complex lifts

| | Routine lift | Complex / non-routine lift |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Examples** | Standard precast, repeat steel at same radius | Tandem cranes, unknown weight, lift over occupied areas |
| **Plan** | May sit in RAMS | Written lift plan reviewed by appointed person |
| **Permit** | Shift or configuration expiry | Reissue if radius, load or weather changes |
| **Audit question** | "Was this within the standard method?" | "Who signed the plan for this configuration?" |

BS 7121 and industry guidance distinguish routine from non-routine lifts. The permit should state which category applies. Complex lifts need a written plan reviewed before issue. Change the radius, reissue.

## Appointed person, operator and slinger roles

The **appointed person** plans the lift and takes professional responsibility for the method. The **operator** controls the machine within that plan. **Slinger/signallers** attach and direct loads; their signals are part of the permitted method.

The permit authorises the team named on it. Swapping a slinger without updating the permit is a common near-miss source.

## Examination records and defects

LOLER thorough examination is not the same as PUWER inspection, though sites often bundle checks. The permit must confirm:

- Exam date valid for the crane/hoist/accessory in use
- Any defects from the last report closed out or mitigated
- Accessories (slings, spreaders) individually traceable

Digital systems can block permit issue when an exam date lapses. Paper systems rely on someone noticing a sticker in the cab.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does LOLER apply to small site hoists and telehandlers?**
Yes, where they are lifting equipment under LOLER. Always check whether the task is a lift or simply handling; the regulations apply to lifting operations.

**Who can sign a lifting operations permit?**
Usually the lift supervisor or site manager acting on the appointed person's plan, not someone with no lifting competence.

**How often must lifting equipment be thoroughly examined?**
Typically every 6 or 12 months depending on equipment type and use, and after exceptional circumstances. See LOLER and your exam schedule.

**Is a lift plan the same as a permit?**
No. The plan is the engineering; the permit authorises execution on a given shift with named plant and people.

**What if wind increases during the lift?**
Stop, secure the load safely if possible, reassess against the plan limits. Do not continue because the permit is still open.
