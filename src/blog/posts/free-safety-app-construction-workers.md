Pick up a brochure from nearly any UK construction safety software company and look at the pricing. It usually works one of two ways. Either a per-user fee across the board, typically £8-£15 per worker per month. Or a tiered model that caps the number of users at the lower tiers and pushes large sites into enterprise pricing.

Either way, the workforce costs money to onboard. On a large civil project with 150 operatives, that's £15,000-£22,500 a year just to let workers sign things on their phone.

This pricing model exists for commercial reasons that make sense to the software company. It doesn't make sense for the contractor. And it's the single biggest reason **a free safety app for construction workers** is worth looking at seriously.

## Why most UK safety software charges per worker

The per-user subscription model is standard SaaS pricing. It scales revenue with customer size, it's simple to quote, and it's easy to forecast. For software in general, it works.

For construction safety software specifically, it creates problems.

Construction workforces are fluid. A site might have 20 operatives one week and 80 the next when a new phase starts. Per-user pricing means the licence count has to be updated constantly, or over-provisioned (paying for seats nobody uses), or under-provisioned (workers can't access the system when they arrive).

Subcontractor crews come and go. A specialist crew on site for three weeks shouldn't require three months of licence payments just to acknowledge toolbox talks during their visit.

The cost per worker also means there's constant pressure to limit who gets access. Supervisors share accounts. Workers sign briefings on a colleague's phone. The software gets used by fewer people than should use it. Adoption collapses from the bottom up.

The platforms that charge per-user also tend to charge per-feature. Worker access to permits is one module. Access to induction is another. Access to toolbox talks is a third. A single subscription turns into multiple line items, and the cost per worker quietly doubles.

## What happens when worker accounts cost money

The consequences of charging for worker access play out the same way on most sites.

**Fewer workers get accounts than should.** The office tries to limit licences to supervisors and key operatives. Other workers get paper alternatives or a shared device.

**Shared accounts undermine the audit trail.** If three workers sign in using one account, the signature on the permit doesn't actually identify who was there.

**Subcontractors get excluded.** The principal contractor pays for their own workforce but not for subbies. The subbies use paper. The evidence chain fragments.

**New starters have delays.** Workers arrive on site, but their account hasn't been provisioned yet. They either delay starting or get brought onto the system later with backdated records.

**The software becomes a supervisor tool, not a workforce tool.** Permits, toolbox talks and induction all get filtered through the supervisor because that's who has the licence. The worker's engagement with the system drops to zero.

All of this is invisible on a sales call. The vendor demonstrates the app, the supervisor sees the workflow, the admin signs the contract. Three months in, the adoption data shows most features being used by 5% of the workforce, the people with paid seats.

## Common objections and the honest answers

**"Free worker accounts sound too good to be true."**
The commercial logic is straightforward. Admin and supervisor tiers generate the revenue. Workers are the consumers of the content the system produces. Limiting consumer access damages the product value without adding meaningful revenue.

**"What stops someone registering their entire company as workers only?"**
Admin and supervisor functions (approving permits, configuring the system, running reports, exporting records) require paid tiers. A company can't operate the platform effectively without at least one paid admin. Large organisations typically need multiple supervisors.

**"Are the worker accounts limited in what they can do?"**
No artificial feature limits. Workers can view and sign everything relevant to them, permits, toolbox talks, induction content, RAMS. What they can't do is approve documents, configure settings or access records outside their scope. That's a role distinction, not a payment distinction.

**"Will free accounts get withdrawn in future?"**
Free worker accounts are part of the product's core design, not a promotional tier. Pricing changes are always possible in software, but the worker-free principle is a deliberate structural choice.
