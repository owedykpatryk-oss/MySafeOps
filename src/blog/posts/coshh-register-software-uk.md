Nearly every UK construction site uses hazardous substances. Concrete and mortar, sealants and adhesives, fuel, solvents, cleaning chemicals, welding fumes. Most of them are well understood and properly controlled. But the paperwork around them, the COSHH register, the assessments, the safety data sheets, is where most sites quietly fall short.

This is where **COSHH register software UK** earns its place. Not by making the substances safer. They're as dangerous or safe as they always were. What it does is make the information accessible at the point of work, current when someone needs it, and provable when an audit arrives.

## What COSHH actually requires

The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH) set out the duty of UK employers to protect workers from exposure to hazardous substances. The regulations apply to any employer whose workers might encounter substances classified as hazardous, which on a construction site is essentially every employer.

The core duties are straightforward:

**Assess the risks.** For each hazardous substance and each task involving it, assess who could be exposed, how, and what the likely exposure level is.

**Prevent or control exposure.** Use the hierarchy of controls: eliminate the substance if possible, substitute for something less hazardous, engineer out the exposure, use administrative controls, and finally PPE.

**Maintain control measures.** Don't just install a dust extraction system. Service it, test it, document it.

**Monitor exposure where required.** Certain substances (silica, welding fumes, isocyanates) have specific workplace exposure limits under HSE's EH40 document.

**Provide health surveillance where appropriate.** Specific medical monitoring for certain substances.

**Provide information, instruction and training.** Workers need to know what they're working with and how to protect themselves.

**Plan for emergencies.** Spills, accidental release, fire involving the substance.

The COSHH register is the document that ties all of this together. It's the evidence trail that proves the duties are being met.

## What a COSHH register contains

A proper **COSHH register** for a UK construction site contains:

- A list of every hazardous substance on site
- The safety data sheet (SDS) for each substance, current version
- A COSHH assessment for each substance and each task it's used for
- Control measures in place (engineering, administrative, PPE)
- Workplace exposure limits where applicable
- Health surveillance requirements where applicable
- Emergency arrangements for spills or accidental release
- Records of monitoring and maintenance of controls

On a small site with three substances, this is a slim folder. On a large construction project with dozens of substances used by multiple subcontractors, it's a substantial body of paperwork, and a substantial body of work to keep current.

This is where the weak spots emerge.

## Why paper COSHH registers go stale

The COSHH register starts neat. By project end, it usually doesn't.

**Safety data sheets go out of date.** Manufacturers update them. New classifications emerge. The version in the binder was current on day one and outdated by week four.

**New substances arrive without being added.** A subcontractor brings something new to site. The substance gets used. The register isn't updated for weeks, if at all.

**Old assessments don't get retired.** Substances stop being used but the assessments stay, making the register look larger than it is.

**Workers can't find the register.** The folder is in the site office. The worker is at the back of the site. The substance is being used right now. Nobody walks back to the cabin to check the SDS.

**Control measures aren't documented as they change.** A new local exhaust ventilation unit goes in. The register still references the old one. An assessment becomes inaccurate.

None of these are dramatic failures. They're slow drift. By the time an audit or an incident highlights the problem, months of incorrect documentation have accumulated.

## What COSHH register software should do

Decent **COSHH register software** addresses the drift directly. Key features worth checking:

**Substance library with current SDS.** The software should link to or host current safety data sheets for common UK construction substances, with the ability to upload custom SDS for specialist materials.

**Task-based assessments.** COSHH isn't just about the substance. It's about how the substance is used. Good software lets you create assessments per task-substance combination, the same paint used for brush application versus spray application are different COSHH events.

**Version control.** When an SDS is updated or an assessment revised, the history should be preserved. This is important for long-running projects and for client audits.

**Mobile access at the point of work.** A COSHH register that only works on a desktop in the site office is the same problem as a paper register in a folder. Workers need to access it where they use the substance.

**Link to work permits.** If a permit is needed for work involving the substance, the COSHH assessment should be accessible from the permit, and vice versa.

**Emergency procedures surfaced.** If a spill happens, the response procedure should be one tap away, not buried in the SDS.

**Review reminders.** COSHH assessments should be reviewed when circumstances change and at minimum annually. The software should prompt for review at sensible intervals.

## Common mistakes with COSHH on construction sites

Sites that struggle with COSHH tend to share a few failings.

**Treating the register as a compliance exercise.** A COSHH folder that nobody reads is a prop, not a control. The register needs to be used in daily work, looked at before using a substance, checked when a spill happens, referenced when briefing new workers.

**One assessment per substance, not per task.** Using concrete admixture in a mixer is different from brushing sealant onto joints. Generic substance-level assessments miss the task-specific controls that actually matter.

**Outdated safety data sheets.** A 2018 SDS on a 2026 register isn't a record, it's a liability. Manufacturers update SDS when classifications or controls change, and using the old version means using out-of-date control advice.

**Missing subcontractor substances.** The principal contractor's register has their own substances but not the subbies'. A proper register covers every substance on site, regardless of who brought it.

**Skipping the emergency arrangements.** Most COSHH assessments are good on normal use. Many are weak on what to do when something goes wrong. Spills, ingestion, eye contact, these need to be as documented as the normal control measures.

**No link to health surveillance.** Substances that require health surveillance (silica dust, isocyanates, some solvents) need monitoring records tied to the COSHH assessment. Platforms that keep these separate create gaps.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a COSHH register legally required in the UK?**
The COSHH Regulations 2002 require risk assessments, but don't mandate a register by name. In practice, the register is the standard means of documenting the assessments and evidence HSE inspectors expect to see.

**Do I need a COSHH assessment for every substance on site?**
For every hazardous substance, yes. Non-hazardous substances don't require a COSHH assessment, though you should check the SDS to confirm classification.

**How often should COSHH assessments be reviewed?**
When circumstances change (new substance, new task, changed controls) and at minimum annually. Review is also prompted by incidents, near-misses and updated safety data sheets.

**Who can write a COSHH assessment?**
A competent person with sufficient knowledge of the substance, the task and the control measures. For complex substances, this might be a health and safety professional. For straightforward substances, a trained supervisor is often sufficient.

**Are digital COSHH registers accepted by HSE inspectors?**
Yes, provided they contain the same information as a paper register and the records can be produced when requested. HSE inspectors increasingly expect digital records on modern UK construction sites.
