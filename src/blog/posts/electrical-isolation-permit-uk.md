# Electrical Isolation Permit UK: LOTO and Live Work in Practice

*By the MySafeOps team · Last updated 7 June 2026 · 7 min read*

> **Key takeaways**
> - The Electricity at Work Regulations 1989 require work on electrical systems to be planned so danger from electricity is prevented.
> - An **electrical isolation permit** (lock-out tag-out) records who isolated what, how dead was proved, and who authorised work.
> - Lock-out beats tag-out alone: a padlock on the isolator with a unique key held by the person in control.
> - Proving dead means test on the equipment to be worked on, not only at the isolator handle.
> - Live work is the exception; dead work with a signed isolation permit is the UK construction norm.

08:10. Plant room. The spark has his permit signed. The MCB is down. The lock is on the wrong isolator. The circuit is still live on the other side of the busbar. The supervisor asks "have you proved dead?" and the tester is still in the van.

That is why UK sites use an **electrical isolation permit UK** teams treat seriously. The Electricity at Work Regulations 1989 (EAWR) do not use the word "permit", but they require systems of work that prevent danger from electrical equipment. On construction sites, a lock-out tag-out (LOTO) permit is how principal contractors and clients expect you to show isolations, tests and authorisations happened before fingers reach conductors.

Electrical isolation sits in the same stack as [hot work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk), [confined space](https://mysafeops.com/blog/confined-space-permit-uk) and [height work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/height-work-permit-uk) inside a [permit to work app](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk). The hazard here is stored energy and unexpected re-energisation, not fire or falls.

## When you need an electrical isolation permit

Issue an isolation permit when work could expose people to live conductors or stored energy unless controls are verified first. Typical triggers on UK building and civils sites:

- Distribution board or panel work (new circuits, alterations, fault finding)
- Isolation of supplies to plant, lifts, HVAC, fire systems, temporary builders' supplies
- Cable jointing, termination or tracing near energised switchgear
- Work on street lighting, traffic signals or site temporary power
- Removal or alteration of lock-out after [hot work](https://mysafeops.com/blog/hot-work-permit-uk) near electrical enclosures
- Reinstatement after isolation (commissioning, energisation handover)

Routine plug-in work on de-energised portable tools from a verified dead socket may sit in RAMS alone. Where multiple trades share the board, isolation points are unclear, or the installation is unfamiliar, use a permit.

## RAMS vs isolation permit vs hot work

| | RAMS | Electrical isolation permit | Hot work permit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Purpose** | Plan the task and hazards | Authorise isolation and dead work today | Authorise heat/sparks and fire watch |
| **Focus** | Method and controls in general | LOTO points, proving dead, authorisation | Combustibles, fire watch, close-out |
| **Typical trigger** | Any non-trivial electrical task | Access to conductors or live zones | Welding, grinding near electrics |
| **Audit question** | "Was there a safe system?" | "Who proved dead and when?" | "Was fire watch closed properly?" |

A RAMS without a permit can suffice for simple, repetitive dead work with clear isolation. A permit without a RAMS is a lock on the wrong device with no method behind it.

## What the permit must record

A usable **electrical isolation permit** is more than "electrician on site". Minimum fields:

**Location and task.** Board reference, circuit number, equipment tag, grid or room reference.

**Isolation points.** Exact device isolated (MCB, switch-disconnector, main isolator), lock number, who holds the key.

**Authorised person.** Named competent person who approved the isolation (often ECS Gold Card holder or client-appointed electrician).

**Operatives.** Who will work on the equipment; competence (ECS, site electrical rules).

**Proving dead.** Instrument type and serial, test on known live before and after, dead test on conductors to be touched.

**Stored energy and adjacent live parts.** Capacitors, UPS, back-feed from other supplies, segregated live sections in the same enclosure.

**Re-energisation plan.** Who removes locks, in what order, and how other trades are cleared.

**Duration and handback.** Start, extension rules, close-out confirming covers replaced and locks removed by named person only.

Digital permits help at shift handover: the incoming supervisor sees open isolations and lock holders instead of chasing keys in pockets.

## Proving dead and test equipment

EAWR and HSE guidance (including HSG85 for low-voltage work) expect you to treat conductors as live until proved dead. The sequence matters:

1. Identify the correct isolation point (not guess from labels alone).
2. Switch off and secure off (lock where possible).
3. Test your voltage indicator on a known live source.
4. Prove dead on the conductors or terminals to be touched.
5. Test the indicator on live again to confirm the tester still works.
6. Record results on the permit.

Use an approved voltage indicator suitable for the system voltage, not a multimeter alone unless your site rules allow it. GS38-style probes and correct category rated kit belong in the brief.

## Live work vs dead work

Dead work with lock-out is the default on UK construction. **Live work** needs a separate justification: why dead work is not reasonably practicable, who is competent, what PPE and barriers apply, and what emergency response exists. Many principal contractors ban live work except for defined client rules.

If live work is authorised, do not fold it into a generic isolation permit without explicit live-work fields. The controls are different. The audit trail should show the exception was deliberate, not accidental.

## Common failures HSE sees

- **Lock on the handle, circuit still fed** from another source or busbar coupling.
- **Proving dead only at the switch** without testing at the work point.
- **Shared keys or missing padlocks** so someone else can re-energise.
- **Tags without locks** on construction sites where multiple trades access boards.
- **Isolation removed before handback** signed; covers off while still energised elsewhere.
- **Permit signed by someone off site** who never saw the board.

On a fit-out in Birmingham, the permit showed isolation complete but the lock was on a spare MCB slot while the feed remained through a bypass link. That is the kind of gap a photo of the locked device on issue catches.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is an electrical isolation permit legally required in the UK?**
The Electricity at Work Regulations 1989 do not name a permit, but they require systems of work that prevent danger from electrical equipment. On UK construction sites, an isolation permit or lock-out tag-out record is the usual way to prove isolations, tests and authorisations happened before work started.

**What is the difference between lock-out and tag-out?**
Lock-out physically prevents a device being re-energised, usually with a padlock on the isolator. Tag-out is a warning label without a lock. UK best practice for construction is lock-out with a unique key held by the person in control, plus tags that name who isolated and when.

**Who can authorise an electrical isolation permit?**
A competent authorised person who understands the installation and the method, often a qualified electrician or appointed electrical supervisor. The person doing the work should not be the sole authoriser of their own isolation.

**How do you prove a circuit is dead before work starts?**
Isolate at a clearly identified point, secure with lock and tag, then test with a suitable approved voltage indicator on the equipment to be worked on. Test the tester on a known live source before and after the dead test. Record the result on the permit.

**Can live electrical work be covered by the same permit?**
Live work needs a separate assessment and usually a specific live-work authorisation with tighter controls. Most construction tasks should be done dead. If live work is unavoidable, the permit must name the justification, competence, PPE and emergency response.
