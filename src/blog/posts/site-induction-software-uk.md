Site inductions are one of those things that nearly every UK construction site gets nearly right. A laminated pack, a 20-minute walk through the key risks, a signature on the attendance sheet. Job done. Except: ask the same worker three weeks later what the site assembly point is, and the answer's often a shrug.

This is the point of **site induction software.** Not to replace the supervisor doing the briefing, that conversation still matters. But to anchor the content somewhere the worker can come back to, and to give the principal contractor an evidence trail that doesn't depend on a folder in the site cabin.

Once workers are inducted, ongoing briefings and [digital toolbox talks](https://mysafeops.com/blog/digital-toolbox-talks) continue the instruction duty under CDM 2015, ideally on the same platform as [permits](https://mysafeops.com/blog/permit-to-work-app-uk) so evidence stays in one place.

## What CDM 2015 says about inductions

The Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015 place specific duties on the principal contractor regarding information, briefing and consultation with workers. The induction isn't called out by name, but regulation 13 requires the principal contractor to ensure every worker is provided with a suitable site induction.

What the regulation doesn't specify is the form, length or content. That's left to the principal contractor to determine based on the risks and nature of the project. In practice, HSE and industry guidance has settled around a standard induction covering:

- Site rules and access arrangements
- Site-specific hazards
- Emergency procedures and assembly points
- First aid arrangements
- Welfare facilities
- PPE requirements for the project
- Reporting procedures (near-misses, incidents, concerns)
- Identification of key site personnel

The duty to evidence this induction happened, and what was covered, sits with the principal contractor. This is where most sites feel the pressure, especially on projects with high worker turnover or multiple subcontractors.

## Why paper-based site inductions fail

Paper inductions work fine up to a point. The failure modes are familiar to any UK site manager.

**Records get lost.** The induction folder ends up in three places, not one.

**Signatures are illegible.** Workers sign quickly. Six months later, nobody can read whose name is whose.

**Content isn't tied to the signature.** The signature proves attendance, not understanding. And the specific content delivered isn't preserved, only the form itself.

**New starters arrive in batches.** Inductions get crammed. Supervisors skip sections to save time. The content quality degrades without anyone noticing.

**Subbies bring their own.** Each subcontractor has slightly different induction content. The main contractor's records only cover their own workforce.

**Updates don't propagate.** If the site rules change mid-project, the induction pack often doesn't reflect it for weeks.

These aren't fatal flaws on their own. Added together, they mean inductions become an admin ritual rather than a genuine safety control.

## What site induction software should include

Good **site induction software** handles the same content as paper but removes the failure points. What to look for:

**Modular content.** Core site rules are the same across most projects. Site-specific hazards change. Good software separates these so updates are easy and templates can be reused.

**Video and visual content.** A photograph of the actual site access, a video of the assembly point, a labelled plan of the welfare facilities. These land better than text-only briefings.

**Comprehension check, not just signature.** A short quiz at the end (three to five questions) forces the worker to engage with the content. This is a CDM-aligned approach that several platforms now support.

**QR code access.** Workers should be able to scan a QR code in the site cabin or at the gate and start their induction immediately. No app download, no account creation friction.

**Offline capability.** Workers often induct in a site cabin with patchy signal. The software needs to work.

**Evidence export.** When a client or HSE inspector wants to see induction records, the export should take seconds.

**Integration with daily attendance.** A worker who's not inducted shouldn't appear on the daily attendance sheet. Integration matters here.

## Making induction content actually useful

The biggest failing of most site inductions isn't the delivery. It's the content. Twenty minutes of generic safety platitudes teaches nothing. Twenty minutes of site-specific, concrete information sticks.

What works on a UK site:

**Specific access points and routes.** "Walk down the yellow pedestrian route from the east gate. Do not use the lorry access on the south side." A map with the route highlighted is worth ten paragraphs of prose.

**Named site personnel.** Not job titles. Names. "If you see a problem, find Dave the site manager, blue hi-vis, radio on channel 3." Human faces remembered better than role labels.

**Actual hazards, not theoretical ones.** "The excavation in Zone B is 3 metres deep. Edge protection is in place but don't rely on it. Stay 2 metres back unless your permit says otherwise." Concrete beats generic every time.

**Weather and seasonal context.** A January induction on a Scottish site should cover ice and low light. A July induction should cover heat and hydration. This seasonal layer is often missing from templates.

**Recent near-misses.** If a scaffold near-miss happened last week, the induction should mention it. This tells the worker the site takes safety seriously and teaches something they'll actually remember.

Good digital induction software makes this level of customisation fast. Bad software makes it impossible.

## Common mistakes with digital inductions

Sites moving from paper to digital inductions tend to fall into predictable traps.

**Treating induction as a one-off signature.** The induction is the start of the evidence trail, not the end of it. A worker who's inducted but then never signs a toolbox talk or permit is evidence that something's wrong.

**Letting the content get generic.** Software makes it easy to reuse induction content across projects. That's a feature, but it's also a trap. Every project has specific hazards that should be briefed specifically.

**Rushing the rollout.** A platform that takes three months to configure is losing months of induction records. Platforms that can be live in a week, with basic content in place, deliver value faster.

**Ignoring the comprehension check.** A signature without a comprehension question is just a tick-box. The check doesn't have to be complex, five multiple-choice questions is enough to force engagement.

**Not integrating with the rest of the site system.** Induction records that sit separately from permits, toolbox talks and attendance create exactly the fragmented evidence CDM tries to avoid.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is site induction legally required under CDM 2015?**
Yes. Regulation 13 places a duty on the principal contractor to provide a suitable site induction to every worker. The regulation doesn't specify the form or length, but the evidence of delivery is expected.

**Can subcontractors deliver their own site induction?**
The principal contractor remains responsible for ensuring every worker on site has received a suitable induction covering site-specific information. Subcontractors can contribute to the content, but the principal contractor needs to verify delivery.

**How long should a site induction be?**
Typically 20 to 45 minutes. Longer inductions lose attention. Shorter ones often miss site-specific content. The length should match the complexity of the project and the risks involved.

**Do workers need to re-induct on long projects?**
Good practice is to re-induct when site conditions change significantly, major phase changes, new zones opening, updated site rules. On very long projects (over 12 months), many principal contractors do annual refreshers.

**Can site inductions be delivered remotely?**
Partial delivery remotely works (policy, rules, general hazards), but site-specific content and the physical tour benefit from in-person delivery. Hybrid inductions, remote pre-briefing, on-site walk-through on day one, are increasingly common on large UK projects.
